Definition & Pathophysiology

Accumulation of air within the pleural space → loss of the negative intrapleural pressure that normally holds the lung expanded against the chest wall → partial or complete lung collapse. Categorized into three types: spontaneous (primary — no underlying lung disease; secondary — underlying lung disease), traumatic (blunt/penetrating injury or iatrogenic), and tension (a one-way valve effect allows air into the pleural space on inspiration but prevents its escape on expiration → progressively rising intrapleural pressure → mediastinal shift → kinking of the great veins → reduced venous return → obstructive shock).

Primary spontaneousTall, thin, young males; smoking strongly associated; rupture of subpleural apical blebs; no underlying lung disease
Secondary spontaneousUnderlying lung disease: COPD, cystic fibrosis, ILD, PJP pneumonia, lung cancer, severe asthma
TraumaticBlunt/penetrating chest trauma, rib fractures; iatrogenic from central line placement, thoracentesis, lung biopsy, positive-pressure ventilation
TensionOne-way valve mechanism; rapidly progressive; obstructive shock physiology — distinguishes from simple pneumothorax
Clinical Features
Classic presentationSudden-onset sharp pleuritic chest pain + acute dyspnea on the affected side
Exam (small, <15%)May be entirely normal; tachycardia often the only finding
Exam (large, >15%)↓ chest wall movement, ipsilateral ↓/absent breath sounds, hyperresonance to percussion, ↓ tactile fremitus
Tension pneumothoraxHypotension, JVD, tracheal deviation AWAY from affected side, severe respiratory distress, absent breath sounds — clinical diagnosis
Diagnostic Workup
Chest X-rayVisible pleural line with absent peripheral lung markings; expiratory films generally not required

Small stable PSP (<3cm)Observation + supplemental O2 (accelerates air resorption up to 4x via nitrogen washout)

Recurrence preventionPleurodesis or VATS with blebectomy for recurrent or persistent air leak
⚠ Tension pneumothorax = obstructive shock. Hypotension + JVD + tracheal deviation + absent breath sounds = needle decompression immediately. Do NOT wait for a chest X-ray — imaging delay can be fatal.
Summary Pearls — Pneumothorax
  • Tall, thin, young male smoker + sudden pleuritic chest pain = primary spontaneous pneumothorax until proven otherwise.
  • Tension pneumothorax is a clinical diagnosis: hypotension, JVD, tracheal deviation away from the affected side, absent breath sounds. Treat first, image later (or not at all before treating).
  • Needle decompression site: 2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line.
  • Secondary spontaneous pneumothorax causes: COPD, cystic fibrosis, ILD, PJP pneumonia, lung cancer, severe asthma.
  • Deep sulcus sign on supine CXR = subtle but specific finding in trauma patients who can't get an upright film.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Chronic inflammatory disorder of the airways characterized by reversible bronchoconstriction, airway hyperresponsiveness, and remodeling. Type 2 (Th2-mediated) inflammation in allergic asthma: allergen exposure → Th2 cells release IL-4, IL-5, IL-13 → IgE production by B cells → mast cell sensitization → re-exposure triggers mast cell degranulation → histamine, leukotrienes, prostaglandins released → bronchoconstriction, mucus hypersecretion, airway edema. Early phase (minutes, bronchospasm) and late phase (4-6 hrs, eosinophilic inflammation) reactions occur. Chronic inflammation → airway remodeling: smooth muscle hypertrophy, subepithelial fibrosis, goblet cell hyperplasia.

Clinical Features
Classic triadEpisodic wheezing, cough (often nocturnal), and dyspnea/chest tightness
TriggersAllergens, exercise, cold air, viral URIs, NSAIDs/aspirin (Samter triad), stress, GERD
ExamExpiratory wheezing, prolonged expiratory phase; in severe attacks, "silent chest" = ominous (no air movement)
Status asthmaticusSevere, prolonged attack unresponsive to bronchodilators; medical emergency — risk of respiratory failure
Samter triadAsthma + aspirin/NSAID sensitivity + nasal polyps — due to shunting of arachidonic acid toward leukotriene pathway when COX is inhibited
Key Distinguishing Features (vs COPD)
ReversibilitySignificant response to bronchodilator: FEV1 increases >12% AND >200mL — hallmark distinguishing feature from COPD
DLCONormal or even ↑ (increased pulmonary capillary blood volume) — contrasts with COPD's ↓DLCO from emphysematous destruction
Age/smoking historyOften younger, atopic history, may be non-smoker (vs COPD which is smoking-associated, older)
EosinophilsSputum and blood eosinophilia common in allergic asthma; Curschmann spirals and Charcot-Leyden crystals on sputum
Diagnostic Workup
Spirometry↓FEV1/FVC ratio <0.7 (obstructive pattern); "scooped out" expiratory flow-volume loop

Step-up therapyAdd LABA (salmeterol, formoterol) to ICS; LTRA (montelukast) as adjunct or for exercise/aspirin-induced asthma
Severe/refractoryBiologics targeting Type 2 inflammation: omalizumab (anti-IgE), mepolizumab/benralizumab (anti-IL-5), dupilumab (anti-IL-4/13)
Acute exacerbationNebulized SABA + ipratropium, systemic corticosteroids, oxygen; epinephrine/magnesium sulfate in severe cases
Samter triad (aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease): asthma + nasal polyps + aspirin/NSAID sensitivity. NSAIDs inhibit COX → arachidonic acid shunted to lipoxygenase pathway → excess leukotrienes → bronchoconstriction. Avoid NSAIDs; leukotriene modifiers (montelukast, zileuton) particularly useful here.
Summary Pearls — Asthma
  • Obstructive pattern: ↓FEV1/FVC <0.7. Reversible with bronchodilator (↑FEV1 >12% and >200mL) — this reversibility is THE key distinguisher from COPD.
  • DLCO is normal or increased in pure asthma (vs decreased in COPD/emphysema) — tests alveolar-capillary integrity which is preserved in asthma.
  • Curschmann spirals (mucus casts of small airways) and Charcot-Leyden crystals (eosinophil membrane protein crystals) on sputum = classic asthma findings.
  • Status asthmaticus with a "silent chest" is an ominous sign — it means too little air is moving to even generate a wheeze, not that the patient has improved.
  • ICS is the controller backbone; SABA is rescue only. Overuse of SABA without controller therapy is a marker of poor control.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Progressive, largely irreversible airflow limitation from two overlapping processes: emphysema (destruction of alveolar walls and interalveolar septa → loss of elastic recoil → air trapping → ↑RV/TLC) and chronic bronchitis (chronic inflammation and mucus gland hyperplasia in the airways → productive cough). Cigarette smoke → activates neutrophils and macrophages → release of proteases (elastase) that overwhelm antiprotease defenses (alpha-1 antitrypsin) → progressive destruction of lung parenchyma. Diagnostic criterion: post-bronchodilator FEV1/FVC <0.70 (GOLD 2024). Smoking cessation is the only intervention proven to slow the rate of FEV1 decline.

Emphysema vs Chronic Bronchitis
Emphysema ("pink puffer")Alveolar wall destruction; ↓DLCO; thin, barrel chest; pursed-lip breathing; minimal cyanosis; weight loss
Chronic bronchitis ("blue bloater")Productive cough ≥3 months/year for 2 consecutive years; cyanosis common; obesity; cor pulmonale risk higher
Centriacinar emphysemaUpper lobe predominant — classic smoking-related pattern
Panacinar emphysemaLower lobe predominant — classic alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency pattern
Clinical Features
Chronic coughOften earliest symptom; worse in morning; productive of mucoid sputum
Progressive dyspneaKey feature, particularly on exertion; gradually progresses to dyspnea at rest
WheezingEspecially during exacerbations
Acute exacerbationOften triggered by respiratory infection; ↑sputum, ↑dyspnea, change in sputum color/purulence
Advanced diseaseHyperinflation, flattened diaphragms, pursed-lip breathing, accessory muscle use, cor pulmonale
Diagnostic Workup
SpirometryPost-bronchodilator FEV1/FVC <0.70 confirms airflow limitation (GOLD 2024); little/no reversibility (vs asthma)
DLCO↓ in emphysema-predominant COPD — reflects alveolar-capillary destruction; helps distinguish from chronic-bronchitis-predominant or asthma
Chest imagingHyperinflation, flattened diaphragms, increased radiolucency; CT shows bullae and emphysematous destruction

ABGMay show chronic respiratory acidosis with metabolic compensation in advanced disease (chronic CO2 retainers)
Treatment
Smoking cessationMost important intervention — only therapy proven to slow FEV1 decline
BronchodilatorsLABA + LAMA (long-acting muscarinic antagonist, e.g., tiotropium) — maintenance therapy backbone
Inhaled corticosteroidsAdded for frequent exacerbators or those with elevated eosinophils; less central role than in asthma
Acute exacerbationBronchodilators + systemic corticosteroids + antibiotics if infectious trigger suspected
Oxygen therapyLong-term home O2 improves survival if resting SpO2 ≤88% or PaO2 ≤55 mmHg
VaccinationAnnual influenza, pneumococcal vaccines — reduce exacerbation risk
SurgicalLung volume reduction surgery or transplant in select advanced cases
A1AT deficiency: young, non-smoker with emphysema → think alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency. Causes panacinar emphysema (lower lobe predominant, vs smoking's centriacinar/upper lobe pattern) AND liver disease (cirrhosis from misfolded protein accumulation in hepatocytes). Autosomal codominant; PiZZ genotype is most severe.
Summary Pearls — COPD
  • FEV1/FVC <0.7 post-bronchodilator with LITTLE TO NO reversibility = COPD (vs significant reversibility in asthma).
  • ↓DLCO in emphysema-predominant COPD (alveolar destruction); asthma has normal/↑DLCO — high-yield distinguishing point.
  • Centriacinar/upper lobe emphysema = smoking. Panacinar/lower lobe emphysema = alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency.
  • ↑RV/TLC (air trapping) + ↑lung compliance + ↑airway resistance = obstructive lung disease physiology.
  • Pink puffer (emphysema) vs blue bloater (chronic bronchitis) — classic but imperfect clinical caricatures; most patients have features of both.
  • Smoking cessation is the only intervention that changes the trajectory of FEV1 decline — pharmacotherapy improves symptoms but not the underlying decline rate.

Definition & Pathophysiology

A group of disorders involving chronic inflammation and progressive fibrosis of the lung interstitium → thickened alveolar walls → impaired gas exchange + reduced lung compliance → restrictive physiology. Persistent alveolar injury (from toxins, immune dysregulation, or unknown cause) → fibroblast activation → excessive collagen deposition → irreversible scarring. Restrictive mechanics: ↓TLC, ↓FVC, but FEV1/FVC ratio is normal or INCREASED (because the stiff, fibrotic lung has enhanced elastic recoil and radial traction on airways, expelling air faster despite the smaller starting volume).

Major Etiologic Categories
Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF)
Most common ILD; unknown cause; UIP pattern on HRCT/biopsy; poor prognosis (median survival ~3 years); diagnosis of exclusion
Pneumoconioses
Asbestosis (lower lobes, ferruginous bodies), silicosis (upper lobes, eggshell calcifications, miners), coal worker's pneumoconiosis
Hypersensitivity pneumonitis
Immune-mediated reaction to inhaled organic antigens — farmer's lung (moldy hay), bird fancier's lung (avian proteins)
Sarcoidosis
Noncaseating granulomas; bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy; African American women; can affect any organ
Connective tissue disease-ILD
RA, scleroderma, dermatomyositis, SLE — systemic symptoms (joint pain, skin changes, muscle weakness) accompany lung findings
Drug-induced
Amiodarone, bleomycin, methotrexate, nitrofurantoin — important medication history
Clinical Features
Progressive dyspneaStarts with exertion, progresses to rest; insidious onset over months to years (except hypersensitivity pneumonitis, which can be acute)
Non-productive coughDry, persistent — classic ILD symptom
Velcro cracklesFine, bibasilar "velcro" rales on auscultation — classic finding, especially IPF
Digital clubbingEspecially advanced IPF
Sarcoidosis stagingCXR Stage I = bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy (BHL); II = BHL + infiltrates; III = infiltrates without BHL; IV = fibrosis
Cor pulmonaleRight heart failure from chronic hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction — late complication
Diagnostic Workup
HRCT chestGold standard imaging. Ground-glass opacities = active inflammation. Honeycombing + reticular opacities + traction bronchiectasis = UIP/IPF pattern
PFTsRestrictive pattern: ↓TLC, ↓FVC, ↑ or normal FEV1/FVC, ↓DLCO (alveolar-capillary interface destruction)
IPF diagnosisUIP pattern on HRCT + exclusion of other known causes (drugs, CTD, environmental exposure) can confirm diagnosis without biopsy
Lung biopsyNeeded if HRCT inconclusive or diagnosis unclear; differentiates UIP from other patterns (NSIP, COP)
SerologiesANA, RF, anti-CCP, myositis panel — screen for underlying connective tissue disease
Treatment
IPFAntifibrotic agents: pirfenidone, nintedanib — slow disease progression (do not reverse fibrosis); lung transplant for eligible candidates
SarcoidosisOften self-limited; corticosteroids (prednisone) first-line only if symptomatic or progressive disease
Hypersensitivity pneumonitisAntigen avoidance is essential; corticosteroids for symptomatic/inflammatory phase
CTD-associated ILDImmunosuppression (corticosteroids, mycophenolate, cyclophosphamide) targeting underlying autoimmune disease
PneumoconiosesAvoid further exposure; supportive care; monitor for complications (TB risk in silicosis, mesothelioma risk in asbestosis)
SupportiveSupplemental oxygen, pulmonary rehabilitation, vaccination
Restrictive lung disease physiology: ↓TLC + ↓FVC + ↑or normal FEV1/FVC ratio (>80%) + ↓DLCO (in intrinsic ILD). The increased FEV1/FVC ratio occurs because increased elastic recoil from fibrosis enhances radial traction on airways, allowing faster expiratory flow rates despite smaller lung volumes — opposite of obstructive disease.
Summary Pearls — Interstitial Lung Disease
  • Restrictive pattern: ↓TLC, ↓FVC, normal/↑FEV1/FVC ratio, ↓DLCO (in intrinsic ILD) — opposite of obstructive disease in nearly every parameter.
  • Honeycombing + subpleural/basilar reticular opacities + traction bronchiectasis on HRCT = UIP pattern, classic for IPF.
  • Velcro crackles (fine bibasilar rales) are the classic auscultation finding in IPF.
  • Sarcoidosis: noncaseating granulomas, bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy, African American women, can mimic many diseases ("the great imitator").
  • Asbestosis = lower lobes + ferruginous bodies + pleural plaques; mesothelioma risk. Silicosis = upper lobes + eggshell calcification of hilar nodes; ↑TB risk (silica impairs macrophage function).
  • Antifibrotics (pirfenidone, nintedanib) SLOW IPF progression — they do not reverse existing fibrosis or cure the disease.
Sleep-Disordered Breathing

Definition & Pathophysiology

Recurrent episodes of partial (hypopnea) or complete (apnea) upper airway obstruction during sleep despite ongoing respiratory effort. Decreased pharyngeal muscle tone during sleep + anatomic airway crowding (obesity, retrognathia, macroglossia, tonsillar hypertrophy, short neck) → collapse of the airway, typically at the level of the soft palate/oropharynx → intermittent hypoxia and hypercapnia → arousal from sleep to reestablish patency → sleep fragmentation. Chronic intermittent hypoxia activates the sympathetic nervous system → hypertension, arrhythmias, atherosclerosis. Chronic hypoxia → ↑HIF → ↑EPO → secondary polycythemia.

Clinical Features
Loud snoringWitnessed apneic episodes by bed partner — classic history
Excessive daytime sleepinessFrom sleep fragmentation; ↑risk of motor vehicle accidents
Morning headacheFrom nocturnal hypercapnia/hypoxia
Risk factorsObesity (strongest), male sex, large neck circumference, retrognathia/micrognathia, tonsillar hypertrophy, acromegaly, hypothyroidism
Cardiovascular sequelaeSystemic hypertension (often resistant), pulmonary hypertension, atrial fibrillation, secondary polycythemia
Key Distinguishing Features
OSA vs Central Sleep ApneaOSA = continued respiratory EFFORT against an obstructed airway. Central = absent respiratory effort (brainstem failure to signal breathing, e.g., heart failure, opioids)
OSA vs Obesity Hypoventilation Syndrome (OHS)OSA: normal daytime ABG. OHS: daytime hypercapnia AND hypoxemia (chronic CO2 retention even while awake) — a more severe overlapping condition
AHI severity gradingMild: 5-15/hr. Moderate: 15-30/hr. Severe: >30/hr (apnea-hypopnea index = events per hour of sleep)
Diagnostic Workup
Polysomnography (PSG)Gold standard — in-lab sleep study measuring airflow, respiratory effort, oxygen saturation, EEG, EMG
Home sleep apnea testSimplified out-of-lab alternative for patients with high pretest probability and no major comorbidities
STOP-BANG questionnaireValidated screening tool: Snoring, Tiredness, Observed apnea, Pressure (HTN), BMI, Age, Neck circumference, Gender
Diagnostic criteriaAHI ≥5/hr with symptoms (sleepiness, snoring) OR AHI ≥15/hr regardless of symptoms
Treatment
First-lineCPAP (continuous positive airway pressure) — pneumatic splint keeps airway open; most effective therapy
Weight lossReduces airway crowding from adipose tissue; can be curative in some patients
Oral appliancesMandibular advancement devices — alternative for mild-moderate OSA or CPAP-intolerant patients
Positional therapyAvoid supine sleep position if apnea is position-dependent
SurgicalUvulopalatopharyngoplasty (UPPP), tonsillectomy (esp. children), hypoglossal nerve stimulation — reserved for refractory cases
AvoidAlcohol, sedatives, opioids before sleep — worsen airway muscle tone and respiratory drive
Untreated OSA → chronic intermittent hypoxia → sympathetic activation → systemic hypertension (often resistant to standard antihypertensives), pulmonary hypertension, atrial fibrillation, and secondary polycythemia (via HIF → EPO pathway). Always screen resistant hypertension patients for OSA.
Summary Pearls — Sleep Apnea
  • OSA = airway obstruction with continued respiratory effort. Central sleep apnea = no respiratory effort (CNS/brainstem problem).
  • OSA has normal daytime ABG; Obesity Hypoventilation Syndrome has daytime hypercapnia AND hypoxemia — key Summary distinguisher.
  • Chronic hypoxia → ↑HIF → ↑EPO → secondary polycythemia (↑Hgb) — classic exam association.
  • CPAP is first-line and most effective treatment; weight loss is the only intervention that can be curative.
  • Obesity is the strongest risk factor; screen resistant hypertension and unexplained atrial fibrillation for OSA.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Infection of the lung parenchyma causing inflammation of the alveolar spaces. Typical pneumonia: bacteria reach the alveoli and trigger an intense neutrophilic inflammatory response → alveolar consolidation, impaired gas exchange. Atypical pneumonia: pathogens (Mycoplasma, Chlamydophila, Legionella, viruses) primarily affect the interstitium and alveolar walls → diffuse inflammation with milder, more gradual clinical presentation ("walking pneumonia"). Aspiration pneumonia: oropharyngeal or gastric contents inhaled into the lungs (risk factors: altered mental status, alcohol use, dysphagia, GERD) → often involves anaerobic oral flora; classically affects the right lower lobe due to the more vertical, wider right mainstem bronchus.

Summary Pearls — Pneumonia
  • Typical = lobar consolidation, productive cough, high fever, sudden onset. Atypical = interstitial pattern, dry cough, gradual onset, "walking pneumonia."
  • S. pneumoniae = most common CAP cause overall, rust-colored sputum. Klebsiella = alcoholics/diabetics, currant jelly sputum, upper lobe cavitation.
  • Mycoplasma → cold agglutinins (IgM) → hemolytic anemia; young adults in close quarters.
  • Legionella → water source outbreaks, hyponatremia, GI symptoms, urinary antigen test.
  • Aspiration pneumonia classically affects the RIGHT lower lobe — due to the right mainstem bronchus being wider, shorter, and more vertical than the left.
  • Post-influenza bacterial pneumonia → think Staphylococcus aureus.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Hearing loss resulting from a disruption in the transmission of sound waves through the external ear canal or middle ear (ossicular chain) before they reach the cochlea. The outer and middle ear normally mediate "conductive" hearing — any blockage, perforation, fluid, or fixation along this pathway impairs sound from ever reaching the functioning inner ear. Because the cochlea and auditory nerve remain intact, the inner ear becomes relatively MORE sensitive to bone-conducted sound, which is the physiologic basis for the Weber test lateralizing to the affected ear.

Common Causes
Cerumen impactionMost common reversible cause; simple wax blockage of the external canal
Otitis media with effusion"Glue ear" — most common cause of conductive hearing loss in children; fluid behind an intact TM impedes ossicular movement
Otitis externaExternal canal swelling/debris from infection (often Pseudomonas) obstructs sound transmission
TM perforationTrauma, barotrauma, or chronic infection — disrupts normal sound-pressure amplification
OtosclerosisAbnormal bone remodeling fixes the stapes footplate to the oval window → failure to transmit sound from stapes to inner ear; classic cause in young adults, progressive, often bilateral
CholesteatomaAbnormal keratinized squamous epithelium growth in the middle ear — erodes ossicles and can destroy surrounding bone
Key Distinguishing Features
Rinne testNEGATIVE on the affected ear — bone conduction (BC) is perceived as louder/longer than air conduction (AC); normal Rinne is positive (AC>BC)
Weber testLateralizes TO the affected (worse-hearing) ear — because conductive loss relatively amplifies bone-conducted sound on that side
Audiogram patternAir-bone gap present — bone conduction thresholds normal, air conduction thresholds elevated
OtoscopyMay show visible pathology: TM perforation, effusion, cerumen, cholesteatoma — unlike SNHL where the exam is often normal
Diagnostic Workup
OtoscopyFirst-line — visualize canal and TM for obstruction, perforation, effusion, infection
Weber and Rinne testsBedside tuning fork tests (512 Hz) to classify conductive vs sensorineural and lateralize
Pneumatic otoscopyAssesses TM mobility — reduced mobility suggests effusion or ossicular fixation
TympanometryObjective measure of TM compliance/middle ear pressure — confirms effusion, perforation, or ossicular chain abnormality
AudiometryDefinitive diagnosis — demonstrates air-bone gap
CT temporal boneFor suspected otosclerosis, cholesteatoma, or chronic disease — evaluates bony anatomy
Treatment
Cerumen impactionCerumenolytics or manual/irrigation removal
Otitis media w/ effusionObservation (often resolves); myringotomy + tympanostomy tubes if persistent >3 months or affecting development
Otitis externaTopical antibiotic/antifungal drops; avoid water exposure
TM perforationMany heal spontaneously; tympanoplasty if persistent
OtosclerosisStapedectomy/stapedotomy (surgical replacement of fixed stapes) — highly effective; hearing aids as alternative
CholesteatomaSurgical excision required — risk of erosion into inner ear, facial nerve, or intracranial structures if untreated
Young adult with progressive, often bilateral conductive hearing loss + normal otoscopy + family history = otosclerosis until proven otherwise. Classic boards vignette: a "reddish hue" (Schwartze sign) may be seen behind the TM. Treated surgically with stapedectomy.
Summary Pearls — Conductive Hearing Loss
  • Rinne negative (BC>AC) on the affected side + Weber lateralizes TO the affected ear = conductive hearing loss.
  • Air-bone gap on audiogram is the objective confirmatory finding.
  • Otitis media with effusion ("glue ear") is the most common cause of conductive hearing loss in children.
  • Otosclerosis: young adult, progressive, often bilateral, normal otoscopy, family history — treat with stapedectomy.
  • Cholesteatoma requires surgery — it's locally destructive and can erode into the facial nerve canal or intracranially.
Otologic Disease · Inner Ear/Nerve

Definition & Pathophysiology

Hearing loss caused by dysfunction of the inner ear (cochlea — sensory) or the auditory/vestibulocochlear nerve (CN VIII) and its central pathways (neural). Because sound is normally transmitted via air conduction more efficiently than bone conduction in a healthy ear, and SNHL affects the cochlea/nerve equally regardless of conduction route, the AC>BC ratio (Rinne positive) is preserved even on the affected side — but overall hearing is reduced. Sensory loss (cochlear/hair cell damage) is sometimes partially reversible; neural loss (CN VIII or central pathway) is rarely recoverable and may signal a serious underlying lesion such as a cerebellopontine angle tumor.

Summary Pearls — Sensorineural Hearing Loss
  • Rinne positive (AC>BC, normal pattern) + Weber lateralizes AWAY from the affected ear = SNHL.
  • Unilateral progressive SNHL + tinnitus + imbalance → think vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma) → get MRI with contrast. Bilateral = NF2.
  • Episodic vertigo + fluctuating SNHL + tinnitus + ear fullness = Meniere disease (endolymphatic hydrops).
  • Aminoglycosides, loop diuretics, cisplatin, and high-dose aspirin are classic ototoxic drugs causing SNHL.
  • Sudden SNHL = otologic emergency — treat with steroids within 2 weeks; delay = worse prognosis.
  • CMV is the most common infectious cause of congenital SNHL; Connexin 26 mutation is the most common genetic cause.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Inflammation of the middle ear, most often following a viral upper respiratory infection that causes eustachian tube dysfunction → impaired drainage and ventilation of the middle ear → fluid accumulation → secondary bacterial infection (acute otitis media, AOM) or sterile effusion without acute infection (otitis media with effusion, OME — "glue ear"). Children are predisposed because their eustachian tubes are shorter, wider, and more horizontal than adults', impairing effective drainage and allowing nasopharyngeal pathogens easier access to the middle ear.

Summary Pearls — Otitis Media
  • AOM diagnostic requirement: middle ear effusion PLUS signs of acute inflammation (bulging TM). Effusion alone without inflammation = OME, not AOM.
  • S. pneumoniae > H. influenzae > M. catarrhalis is the pathogen frequency order for AOM.
  • Amoxicillin is first-line treatment; high-dose for resistant S. pneumoniae coverage.
  • OME ("glue ear") is the most common cause of conductive hearing loss in children — tympanostomy tubes if persistent beyond 3 months.
  • Mastoiditis = postauricular swelling/erythema + fever in a child with AOM — true emergency requiring IV antibiotics ± surgery.
  • Children's short, wide, horizontal eustachian tubes explain their predisposition to otitis media compared to adults.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Autosomal recessive disorder from mutations in the CFTR gene (chromosome 7), encoding a cAMP-regulated chloride channel on epithelial cells. Defective CFTR → decreased chloride (and bicarbonate) secretion + increased sodium/water reabsorption across epithelial surfaces → thick, dehydrated, viscous secretions in the lungs, pancreas, GI tract, sweat glands, and reproductive tract. In the lungs: mucus accumulation impairs mucociliary clearance → recurrent infections (Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Staphylococcus aureus, Burkholderia cepacia) → chronic inflammation → bronchiectasis and progressive fibrosis. The most common mutation, ΔF508 (Class II), causes a misfolded CFTR protein that is degraded before reaching the cell surface.

CFTR modulator therapy (Trikafta and related drugs) represents a paradigm shift — these drugs target the underlying molecular defect rather than just managing symptoms, and have meaningfully extended life expectancy for eligible CF patients with responsive genotypes (including ΔF508 heterozygotes/homozygotes).
Summary Pearls — Cystic Fibrosis
  • CFTR gene on chromosome 7 — cAMP-regulated chloride channel. ΔF508 (Class II, misfolding) is the most common mutation.
  • Sweat chloride ≥60 mEq/L = diagnostic. This is THE classic boards lab value to memorize.
  • Meconium ileus in a newborn = think CF until proven otherwise — classic first presentation.
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa chronic colonization is the hallmark CF lung pathogen and major driver of decline.
  • Congenital bilateral absence of the vas deferens (CBAVD) causes male infertility in CF — even in mild/atypical CF presentations.
  • End-stage lung disease is the principal cause of death in CF, despite the multisystem nature of the disease.
  • Distinguish from Kartagener/primary ciliary dyskinesia: situs inversus + normal sweat chloride points away from CF.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Malignant neoplasm arising from bronchial or alveolar epithelium, broadly divided into small cell lung cancer (SCLC, ~15%) and non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC, ~85%) — the latter comprising adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and large cell carcinoma. Pathogenesis: carcinogen exposure (tobacco smoke is dominant) → genetic mutations (EGFR, ALK, KRAS oncogene activation; TP53 tumor suppressor inactivation) → uncontrolled epithelial proliferation. Local invasion into bronchi, lung tissue, and chest wall; lymphatic spread to mediastinal nodes; distant metastases classically to liver, brain, bone, and adrenal glands.

Histologic Subtypes
Adenocarcinoma
Most common lung cancer overall, especially in nonsmokers and women; peripheral location; glandular/mucin-producing pattern; EGFR/ALK mutations common, especially in never-smokers — targetable
Squamous cell carcinoma
Strongly linked to smoking; central/hilar location; cavitation common; keratin pearls and intercellular bridges on histology; associated with PTHrP-mediated hypercalcemia
Large cell carcinoma
Less common, poorly differentiated, usually peripheral, rapidly growing; diagnosis of exclusion (lacks glandular or squamous features)
Small cell carcinoma (oat cell)
~15% of cases; strongly linked to smoking; central location; neuroendocrine origin; very aggressive, early metastasis; most frequently associated with paraneoplastic syndromes

Summary Pearls — Lung Cancer
  • Central tumors (cavitation, hilar): squamous cell carcinoma and small cell carcinoma — both strongly smoking-associated.
  • Peripheral tumors: adenocarcinoma (most common overall, especially nonsmokers/women) and large cell carcinoma.
  • SIADH and ectopic ACTH (Cushing) = small cell lung cancer. PTHrP-hypercalcemia = squamous cell carcinoma. Memorize this pairing.
  • Lambert-Eaton: weakness IMPROVES with repetition (presynaptic Ca2+ channel antibodies) — associated with SCLC.
  • Pancoast tumor: apical lung tumor → Horner syndrome (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis) from sympathetic chain invasion.
  • SVC syndrome: facial/neck swelling + distended chest veins from tumor compression — oncologic emergency.
  • EGFR mutations are enriched in adenocarcinoma, especially in never-smoking women of Asian descent — targetable with TKIs.

Definition & Classification Framework

Anemia is a reduction in hemoglobin concentration (WHO: Hgb <12 g/dL in women, <13 g/dL in men) leading to decreased oxygen delivery. The diagnostic approach uses two axes: MCV (cell size) and reticulocyte count (bone marrow response). Summary: Is the reticulocyte count high (destruction/loss) or low (production failure)? Step 2: Use MCV to classify production failures into micro-, normo-, or macrocytic subtypes.

Microcytic (MCV <80 fL)

Mnemonic: TAILS
Thalassemia · Anemia of chronic disease · Iron deficiency · Lead poisoning · Sideroblastic anemia


Mechanism: deficient heme or globin synthesis → small, pale RBCs

Normocytic (MCV 80–100 fL)

Causes: Acute blood loss, hemolytic anemias, anemia of chronic disease (early), aplastic anemia, renal failure (↓EPO), hypothyroidism, bone marrow infiltration


Key: check reticulocyte count to separate high (hemolysis/loss) from low (production failure)

Macrocytic (MCV >100 fL)

Megaloblastic: B12 deficiency, folate deficiency (DNA synthesis defect)
Non-megaloblastic: alcohol, liver disease, hypothyroidism, MDS, reticulocytosis, drugs (MTX, hydroxyurea)

Reticulocyte Count — The Key Branch Point
High reticulocytes (>2–3%) = Appropriate BM response
Bone marrow is working fine but RBCs are being lost or destroyed. Think: hemorrhage (acute blood loss) or hemolysis (intrinsic or extrinsic RBC destruction).
Low reticulocytes (<2%) = Inadequate BM response
Bone marrow is failing to produce enough RBCs. Classify by MCV → microcytic (TAILS), normocytic (aplastic, renal failure), or macrocytic (B12/folate, liver, alcohol).

Definition & Pathophysiology

Thalassemias are inherited autosomal recessive disorders of globin chain synthesis. Reduced production of one chain type → relative excess of the other → excess chains precipitate → damage RBC membrane → hemolysis and ineffective erythropoiesis. Iron studies are normal (iron absorption intact), which is the key distinguisher from iron deficiency anemia. Definition & Pathophysiology

Summary Pearls — Sickle Cell Disease
  • Mutation: GAG→GTG = Glu→Val at position 6 of beta-globin. HbSS = homozygous (disease). HbAS = trait (protective against P. falciparum malaria).
  • Triggers for sickling: Hypoxia, Dehydration, Acidosis, Cold, Infection — remember HDACI.
  • Dactylitis (hand-foot syndrome) = first presentation of SCD in children under 2 years old.
  • Parvovirus B19 → aplastic crisis (shuts down erythroid precursors → sudden severe anemia).
  • Salmonella species → osteomyelitis in SCD (S. aureus still most common in general population).
  • Autosplenectomy by age 5 → Howell-Jolly bodies → give prophylactic penicillin + vaccines (pneumococcal, meningococcal, Hib).
  • Hydroxyurea increases HbF → HbF inhibits HbS polymerization → fewer crises.
  • Acute chest syndrome = leading cause of death in SCD → exchange transfusion is key.

Definition & Life Cycle

Malaria is caused by Plasmodium protozoa transmitted by the bite of female Anopheles mosquitoes. Five species infect humans: P. falciparum, P. vivax, P. ovale, P. malariae, P. knowlesi. Life cycle: (1) female Anopheles injects sporozoites → (2) sporozoites travel to liver → hepatic schizogony (asymptomatic) → (3) liver schizonts rupture, releasing merozoites → (4) merozoites infect RBCs → erythrocytic schizogony → (5) schizont rupture → fever/symptoms + release of new merozoites. P. vivax and P. ovale form dormant hypnozoites in liver → reactivate weeks to years later → relapses.

Summary Pearls — Malaria
  • P. falciparum: most severe; banana-shaped gametocytes; multiple ring forms per RBC; no hypnozoites; cerebral malaria; blackwater fever (hemoglobinuria).
  • P. vivax and P. ovale: hypnozoites in liver → relapses months/years later → primaquine eradicates liver stage. Check G6PD first!
  • P. malariae: quartan fever (72h cycle); nephrotic syndrome; infects senescent RBCs; no hypnozoites.
  • Schüffner's dots on smear = P. vivax or P. ovale. Banana-shaped gametocytes = P. falciparum.
  • Duffy antigen is required for P. vivax RBC invasion → Duffy-negative individuals (West Africans) are naturally resistant to P. vivax.
  • Sickle cell trait and G6PD deficiency are protective against P. falciparum malaria.
  • IV artesunate preferred over IV quinine for severe falciparum (better efficacy, fewer side effects).

Coagulation Cascade Overview
Extrinsic Pathway (PT/INR)
Tissue factor (III) + Factor VII → activates Factor X. "SITS" — Seven Is The Start. PT/INR elevated if factors in this pathway are deficient (VII, X, V, II, fibrinogen). Warfarin blocks vitamin K-dependent factors (II, VII, IX, X, Protein C, S).
Intrinsic Pathway (PTT)
Contact activation → XII → XI → IX → VIII → X. "PET" → PTT tests Eight and nine (IXa + VIIIa activate X). PTT elevated if: hemophilia A (VIII↓), hemophilia B (IX↓), heparin (activates antithrombin → inactivates IIa/Xa). vWF carries VIII — vWD can ↑PTT.
Common Pathway (both PT & PTT)
Factor X → Xa (with Va) → prothrombin (II) → thrombin (IIa) → fibrinogen (I) → fibrin. XIII crosslinks fibrin. Deficiency here affects both PT and PTT.
Primary Hemostasis (Bleeding Time)
Platelet plug formation: vascular injury → vWF → platelet GpIb binding (adhesion) → ADP/TXA2 → platelet aggregation (GpIIb/IIIa). BT elevated in: platelet disorders, vWD, aspirin/NSAIDs, uremia.

Summary Pearls — Coagulation Cascade
  • PT tests extrinsic (VII) and common pathway. PTT tests intrinsic (VIII, IX, XI, XII) and common pathway.
  • Vitamin K-dependent factors: II, VII, IX, X, Protein C, S (mnemonic: "1972" → factors 2,7,9,10).
  • Factor VII has the shortest half-life → PT/INR elevated first in warfarin therapy and liver disease.
  • TTP: ADAMTS13 deficiency → ultralarge vWF multimers → microthrombi → FAT RN pentad: Fever, microAngiopathic hemolytic anemia (schistocytes), Thrombocytopenia, Renal failure, Neurologic changes. Treat with plasmapheresis.
  • DIC: EVERYTHING is consumed → PT↑, PTT↑, fibrinogen↓, platelets↓, D-dimer↑↑. Causes: sepsis, trauma, obstetric complications, malignancy.
  • Mixing study: mix patient plasma with normal plasma. If PTT corrects → factor DEFICIENCY (hemophilia). If PTT does NOT correct → INHIBITOR (autoantibody against factor).

Definition & Pathophysiology

Hemophilia A and B are X-linked recessive disorders — almost exclusively males affected (females are carriers, occasionally symptomatic). Hemophilia A: deficiency of Factor VIII; most common (80% of hemophilias). Hemophilia B (Christmas disease): deficiency of Factor IX. Both factors are in the intrinsic pathway → IXa + VIIIa together activate Factor X. Deficiency → inadequate thrombin generation → failure to form stable fibrin clot → bleeding into deep tissues (joints, muscles). Both are clinically indistinguishable — only factor assay differentiates them. ~1/3 of cases arise from spontaneous mutation (no family history).

Summary Pearls — Hemophilia A & B
  • X-linked recessive — almost exclusively in males. Females are carriers (may have mild symptoms).
  • Classic presentation: young boy with hemarthroses (joint bleeds) + elevated PTT + normal PT + normal bleeding time.
  • Hemophilia A = Factor VIII↓ (most common). Hemophilia B = Factor IX↓ (Christmas disease). Clinically identical — distinguish by factor assay.
  • DDAVP (desmopressin): releases stored vWF and Factor VIII from endothelium → effective for mild Hemophilia A and vWD Type 1. Not useful for B.
  • Mixing study: PTT corrects with normal plasma = factor DEFICIENCY (hemophilia). PTT does NOT correct = INHIBITOR (autoantibody).
  • Acquired hemophilia: autoantibodies against FVIII develop in SLE, pregnancy, malignancy → PTT elevated, mixing study does NOT correct. Treat with corticosteroids + emicizumab.

Other Thrombophilias & Hypercoagulable States
Prothrombin G20210A mutation
2nd most common inherited thrombophilia in Caucasians. Elevated prothrombin levels → increased thrombin generation → DVT/PE risk. Normal PT/PTT. Autosomal dominant.
Protein C & S deficiency
Natural anticoagulants. Deficiency → inadequate inactivation of Va and VIIIa → hypercoagulable. Warfarin-induced skin necrosis (Protein C short half-life, drops first). Autosomal dominant.
Antithrombin III deficiency
AT-III normally inhibits thrombin and Xa. Deficiency → hypercoagulable. Heparin resistance (heparin requires AT-III to work). Acquired by nephrotic syndrome, liver disease, heparin use.
Antiphospholipid syndrome (APS)
Autoimmune: antiphospholipid antibodies → paradoxical hypercoagulable state (thrombosis + recurrent miscarriages) despite ↑PTT in vitro. Lupus anticoagulant; anticardiolipin Ab; anti-β2 glycoprotein I. Treatment: lifelong anticoagulation.
Warfarin-induced skin necrosis: occurs early in warfarin therapy due to rapid depletion of Protein C (short half-life, vitamin K-dependent) before anticoagulant effect is established → paradoxical thrombosis of skin microvasculature → necrosis. Seen especially in Protein C/S deficiency. Overlap with heparin when starting warfarin to prevent this.
Summary Pearls — Acquired Disorders
  • ITP: isolated thrombocytopenia + normal PT/PTT + increased megakaryocytes on marrow. Treat with steroids → IVIG → splenectomy. Do NOT transfuse platelets unless emergency.
  • TTP: pentad (FAT RN) + schistocytes + NORMAL PT/PTT (not DIC!). ADAMTS13 deficiency. Treat with PLASMAPHERESIS — do NOT give platelets.
  • HUS vs TTP: HUS = E. coli O157:H7 (Shiga toxin) + hemolytic anemia + renal failure + thrombocytopenia, primarily children. Treatment: supportive (avoid antibiotics in Shiga-HUS).
  • DIC: ALL consumed (PT↑, PTT↑, fibrinogen↓, platelets↓, D-dimer↑↑). Treat the underlying cause FIRST.
  • Factor V Leiden: most common inherited thrombophilia in Caucasians. Normal PT/PTT. Resistance to APC. DVT/PE/miscarriages. Worsened by OCP.
  • vWD: mucosal bleeding (NOT hemarthroses). ↑bleeding time + ↓ristocetin cofactor activity. DDAVP for Type 1. Most common inherited bleeding disorder overall.
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome: paradox — ↑PTT in vitro but HYPERCOAGULABLE in vivo. Recurrent thrombosis + miscarriages. Treat with lifelong anticoagulation.
