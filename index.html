Definition & Pathophysiology

Malignant clonal proliferation of immature myeloid precursors (blasts) arrested in early differentiation. Acquired somatic mutations → differentiation arrest → non-functional blast accumulation → bone marrow failure. Key mutations: FLT3-ITD (poor prognosis), NPM1 (good), CEBPA double mutant (good). Critical subtype: t(15;17) → PML-RARα → APL (M3) — differentiation arrest at promyelocyte, high DIC risk.

Step 1 Pearls — AML
  • Auer rods = AML (myeloid). No Auer rods in ALL (lymphoid).
  • APL (M3) + DIC + faggot cells → ATRA + ATO. Never start with 7+3.
  • FLT3-ITD = poor prognosis; NPM1 mutated = good prognosis.
  • Gingival hyperplasia → monocytic AML (M4 or M5).
  • AML is TdT−; ALL is TdT+ — key immunophenotypic distinction.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Malignant clonal proliferation of immature lymphoid precursors (lymphoblasts — B or T cell) arrested in early development, replacing normal bone marrow. Key genetic events: t(12;21) ETV6-RUNX1 (25% pediatric, good prognosis); t(9;22) BCR-ABL Philadelphia chromosome (25% adult ALL, poor prognosis); MLL rearrangements (infant ALL, very poor); hyperdiploidy (>50 chr, favorable in children).

Step 1 Pearls — ALL
  • Child + bone pain + lymphadenopathy + TdT+ lymphoblasts = ALL. Most common pediatric cancer.
  • TdT+ = lymphoid immaturity (both B and T cell ALL). TdT− = AML.
  • CNS and testes are sanctuary sites — require intrathecal methotrexate prophylaxis.
  • T-ALL: adolescent male + anterior mediastinal mass + TdT+ = T-ALL; risk of SVC syndrome.
  • t(9;22) in ALL → add TKI. t(12;21) in children → favorable prognosis.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Clonal myeloproliferative disorder of a pluripotent stem cell driven by t(9;22) → BCR-ABL fusion → constitutively active tyrosine kinase → uncontrolled myeloid proliferation with initially intact differentiation. Three phases: Chronic (manageable, years) → Accelerated (10–19% blasts) → Blast crisis (>20% blasts, AML-like or ALL-like, poor prognosis).

Step 1 Pearls — CML
  • t(9;22) Philadelphia chromosome → BCR-ABL constitutive TK → CML. Also in 25% adult ALL.
  • Basophilia is the hallmark peripheral smear finding. Full myeloid spectrum visible.
  • LOW LAP score distinguishes CML from leukemoid reaction (HIGH LAP).
  • Imatinib (Gleevec) = first-line TKI. Converted CML from fatal to manageable disease.
  • Three phases: Chronic → Accelerated → Blast crisis (acute leukemia, poor prognosis).

Definition & Pathophysiology

Monoclonal accumulation of functionally incompetent mature-appearing B lymphocytes that aberrantly co-express CD5 (normally T-cell marker) + CD19, CD20 (dim), CD23. BCL-2 overexpression → defective apoptosis → lymphocyte accumulation → marrow failure + immune dysfunction. Hypogammaglobulinemia → recurrent encapsulated bacterial infections. Autoimmune hemolytic anemia (AIHA) and ITP from dysfunctional autoantibodies. Spectrum with SLL.

Step 1 Pearls — CLL
  • Smudge (basket) cells = CLL. Elderly + painless lymphadenopathy + lymphocytosis + smudge cells.
  • CD5+/CD19+/CD23+ co-expression on B cells is the hallmark immunophenotype.
  • Watch and wait early stage — treatment does not improve survival until symptomatic.
  • Richter transformation: CLL → aggressive DLBCL; sudden B symptoms + rising LDH = emergency.
  • Hypogammaglobulinemia → recurrent infections with encapsulated bacteria (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae).

Definition & Pathophysiology

Malignant clonal plasma cells secrete M protein (IgG 55%, IgA 25%) + excess free light chains (Bence Jones proteins). Plasma cells stimulate osteoclasts via RANKL/IL-6 and suppress osteoblasts via DKK-1 → lytic bone lesions without reactive sclerosis. They crowd normal marrow → cytopenias and suppress normal Ig → immunodeficiency. Bence Jones proteins → nephrotoxic tubular damage → renal failure (myeloma kidney).

CRAB criteria: hyperCalcemia · Renal insufficiency · Anemia · Bone lesions (lytic) — defines symptomatic MM requiring treatment

Step 1 Pearls — Multiple Myeloma

  • CRAB: Calcium↑, Renal failure, Anemia, Bone lesions (LYTIC, punched-out, no sclerosis).

  • Plasma cell morphology: eccentric nucleus + clock-face chromatin + perinuclear hof (Golgi).
  • MGUS → 1% annual risk of progression. No CRAB = observe only.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Malignant lymphoma defined by Reed-Sternberg (RS) cells — large binucleated cells with prominent "owl-eye" eosinophilic nucleoli — expressing CD30+ / CD15+ but lacking CD45 (LCA) and usually CD20. Derived from germinal center B cells. EBV associated with mixed cellularity subtype. RS cells are a minority; surrounding reactive microenvironment drives symptoms via cytokine release (IL-5 → eosinophilia; IL-6 → fever; IL-13 → pruritus). Spreads contiguously (node-to-node, orderly).

Rare; best prognosis; few RS cells
Lymphocyte-depleted
Rare; worst prognosis; HIV-associated; many RS cells

Step 1 Pearls — Hodgkin Lymphoma
  • RS cells: CD30+ / CD15+ / CD45− — large binucleated, owl-eye nucleoli. HL hallmark.
  • HL spreads contiguously (orderly). NHL spreads non-contiguously (disseminated).
  • Alcohol-induced pain at lymph node sites = PATHOGNOMONIC of Hodgkin lymphoma.
  • Nodular sclerosis = most common subtype; young woman + mediastinal mass + lacunar cells.
  • ABVD: Bleomycin → pulmonary toxicity. Adriamycin → cardiomyopathy.

Definition & Classification Framework

Anemia is a reduction in hemoglobin concentration (WHO: Hgb <12 g/dL in women, <13 g/dL in men) leading to decreased oxygen delivery. The diagnostic approach uses two axes: MCV (cell size) and reticulocyte count (bone marrow response). Step 1: Is the reticulocyte count high (destruction/loss) or low (production failure)? Step 2: Use MCV to classify production failures into micro-, normo-, or macrocytic subtypes.

Microcytic (MCV <80 fL)

Mnemonic: TAILS
Thalassemia · Anemia of chronic disease · Iron deficiency · Lead poisoning · Sideroblastic anemia


Mechanism: deficient heme or globin synthesis → small, pale RBCs

Normocytic (MCV 80–100 fL)

Causes: Acute blood loss, hemolytic anemias, anemia of chronic disease (early), aplastic anemia, renal failure (↓EPO), hypothyroidism, bone marrow infiltration


Key: check reticulocyte count to separate high (hemolysis/loss) from low (production failure)

Macrocytic (MCV >100 fL)

Megaloblastic: B12 deficiency, folate deficiency (DNA synthesis defect)
Non-megaloblastic: alcohol, liver disease, hypothyroidism, MDS, reticulocytosis, drugs (MTX, hydroxyurea)

Reticulocyte Count — The Key Branch Point
High reticulocytes (>2–3%) = Appropriate BM response
Bone marrow is working fine but RBCs are being lost or destroyed. Think: hemorrhage (acute blood loss) or hemolysis (intrinsic or extrinsic RBC destruction).
Low reticulocytes (<2%) = Inadequate BM response
Bone marrow is failing to produce enough RBCs. Classify by MCV → microcytic (TAILS), normocytic (aplastic, renal failure), or macrocytic (B12/folate, liver, alcohol).

Step 1 Pearls — Anemia General
  • First branch point: Is the reticulocyte count high (loss/destruction) or low (production failure)?
  • Second branch point (if low retics): What is the MCV? Microcytic → TAILS; Normocytic → aplastic/renal/early IDA; Macrocytic → B12/folate/alcohol/liver.
  • Hemolysis findings: high retics + ↑indirect bilirubin + ↑LDH + ↓haptoglobin.
  • RDW ↑ in iron deficiency and B12/folate deficiency (anisocytosis); RDW normal in thalassemia trait (uniform small cells).
  • Early iron deficiency can be normocytic — MCV drops only after prolonged depletion.

Step 1 Pearls — Microcytic Anemias
  • IDA: ferritin↓ (first to deplete) + TIBC↑ + serum iron↓. Ferritin is the most sensitive early marker.
  • ACD: serum iron↓ but ferritin↑ + TIBC↓ — iron is trapped in macrophages by hepcidin. The key distinguisher from IDA.
  • Sideroblastic anemia: serum iron↑ + ferritin↑ + ringed sideroblasts on bone marrow. INH → give B6. Lead → give DMSA/EDTA.
  • Thalassemia trait: microcytic anemia with NORMAL iron studies + ELEVATED RBC count + target cells on smear. Confirm with Hgb electrophoresis.
  • Basophilic stippling with ringed sideroblasts = sideroblastic anemia. Basophilic stippling WITHOUT ringed sideroblasts = lead poisoning.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Thalassemias are inherited autosomal recessive disorders of globin chain synthesis. Reduced production of one chain type → relative excess of the other → excess chains precipitate → damage RBC membrane → hemolysis and ineffective erythropoiesis. Iron studies are normal (iron absorption intact), which is the key distinguisher from iron deficiency anemia. Compensatory: ↑HbA2 and HbF in beta-thalassemia; bone marrow expands; extramedullary hematopoiesis.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Single nucleotide substitution in beta-globin gene: GAG→GTG → Glutamate→Valine at position 6. HbS under deoxygenated conditions forms insoluble polymers → sickling → (1) vaso-occlusion: rigid sickled RBCs + endothelial adhesion + leukocytes → microvascular occlusion → ischemia/infarction; (2) hemolysis: sickled cells destroyed in spleen → chronic normocytic hemolytic anemia. Triggers: hypoxia, dehydration, acidosis, cold, infection. HbS trait (HbAS) is protective against P. falciparum malaria.

  • Acute chest syndrome = leading cause of death in SCD → exchange transfusion is key.

Definition & Life Cycle

Malaria is caused by Plasmodium protozoa transmitted by the bite of female Anopheles mosquitoes. Five species infect humans: P. falciparum, P. vivax, P. ovale, P. malariae, P. knowlesi. Life cycle: (1) female Anopheles injects sporozoites → (2) sporozoites travel to liver → hepatic schizogony (asymptomatic) → (3) liver schizonts rupture, releasing merozoites → (4) merozoites infect RBCs → erythrocytic schizogony → (5) schizont rupture → fever/symptoms + release of new merozoites. P. vivax and P. ovale form dormant hypnozoites in liver → reactivate weeks to years later → relapses.

Step 1 Pearls — Malaria
  • P. falciparum: most severe; banana-shaped gametocytes; multiple ring forms per RBC; no hypnozoites; cerebral malaria; blackwater fever (hemoglobinuria).
  • P. vivax and P. ovale: hypnozoites in liver → relapses months/years later → primaquine eradicates liver stage. Check G6PD first!
  • P. malariae: quartan fever (72h cycle); nephrotic syndrome; infects senescent RBCs; no hypnozoites.
  • Schüffner's dots on smear = P. vivax or P. ovale. Banana-shaped gametocytes = P. falciparum.
  • Duffy antigen is required for P. vivax RBC invasion → Duffy-negative individuals (West Africans) are naturally resistant to P. vivax.
  • Sickle cell trait and G6PD deficiency are protective against P. falciparum malaria.
  • IV artesunate preferred over IV quinine for severe falciparum (better efficacy, fewer side effects).

Coagulation Cascade Overview
Extrinsic Pathway (PT/INR)
Tissue factor (III) + Factor VII → activates Factor X. "SITS" — Seven Is The Start. PT/INR elevated if factors in this pathway are deficient (VII, X, V, II, fibrinogen). Warfarin blocks vitamin K-dependent factors (II, VII, IX, X, Protein C, S).
Intrinsic Pathway (PTT)
Contact activation → XII → XI → IX → VIII → X. "PET" → PTT tests Eight and nine (IXa + VIIIa activate X). PTT elevated if: hemophilia A (VIII↓), hemophilia B (IX↓), heparin (activates antithrombin → inactivates IIa/Xa). vWF carries VIII — vWD can ↑PTT.
Common Pathway (both PT & PTT)
Factor X → Xa (with Va) → prothrombin (II) → thrombin (IIa) → fibrinogen (I) → fibrin. XIII crosslinks fibrin. Deficiency here affects both PT and PTT.
Primary Hemostasis (Bleeding Time)
Platelet plug formation: vascular injury → vWF → platelet GpIb binding (adhesion) → ADP/TXA2 → platelet aggregation (GpIIb/IIIa). BT elevated in: platelet disorders, vWD, aspirin/NSAIDs, uremia.

Step 1 Pearls — Coagulation Cascade
  • PT tests extrinsic (VII) and common pathway. PTT tests intrinsic (VIII, IX, XI, XII) and common pathway.
  • Vitamin K-dependent factors: II, VII, IX, X, Protein C, S (mnemonic: "1972" → factors 2,7,9,10).

  • Mixing study: mix patient plasma with normal plasma. If PTT corrects → factor DEFICIENCY (hemophilia). If PTT does NOT correct → INHIBITOR (autoantibody against factor).

Definition & Pathophysiology

Hemophilia A and B are X-linked recessive disorders — almost exclusively males affected (females are carriers, occasionally symptomatic). Hemophilia A: deficiency of Factor VIII; most common (80% of hemophilias). Hemophilia B (Christmas disease): deficiency of Factor IX. Both factors are in the intrinsic pathway → IXa + VIIIa together activate Factor X. Deficiency → inadequate thrombin generation → failure to form stable fibrin clot → bleeding into deep tissues (joints, muscles). Both are clinically indistinguishable — only factor assay differentiates them. ~1/3 of cases arise from spontaneous mutation (no family history).

Other Thrombophilias & Hypercoagulable States
Prothrombin G20210A mutation
2nd most common inherited thrombophilia in Caucasians. Elevated prothrombin levels → increased thrombin generation → DVT/PE risk. Normal PT/PTT. Autosomal dominant.
Protein C & S deficiency
Natural anticoagulants. Deficiency → inadequate inactivation of Va and VIIIa → hypercoagulable. Warfarin-induced skin necrosis (Protein C short half-life, drops first). Autosomal dominant.
Antithrombin III deficiency
AT-III normally inhibits thrombin and Xa. Deficiency → hypercoagulable. Heparin resistance (heparin requires AT-III to work). Acquired by nephrotic syndrome, liver disease, heparin use.
Antiphospholipid syndrome (APS)
Autoimmune: antiphospholipid antibodies → paradoxical hypercoagulable state (thrombosis + recurrent miscarriages) despite ↑PTT in vitro. Lupus anticoagulant; anticardiolipin Ab; anti-β2 glycoprotein I. Treatment: lifelong anticoagulation.
Warfarin-induced skin necrosis: occurs early in warfarin therapy due to rapid depletion of Protein C (short half-life, vitamin K-dependent) before anticoagulant effect is established → paradoxical thrombosis of skin microvasculature → necrosis. Seen especially in Protein C/S deficiency. Overlap with heparin when starting warfarin to prevent this.

  • ITP: isolated thrombocytopenia + normal PT/PTT + increased megakaryocytes on marrow. Treat with steroids → IVIG → splenectomy. Do NOT transfuse platelets unless emergency.
  • TTP: pentad (FAT RN) + schistocytes + NORMAL PT/PTT (not DIC!). ADAMTS13 deficiency. Treat with PLASMAPHERESIS — do NOT give platelets.
  • HUS vs TTP: HUS = E. coli O157:H7 (Shiga toxin) + hemolytic anemia + renal failure + thrombocytopenia, primarily children. Treatment: supportive (avoid antibiotics in Shiga-HUS).
  • DIC: ALL consumed (PT↑, PTT↑, fibrinogen↓, platelets↓, D-dimer↑↑). Treat the underlying cause FIRST.
  • Factor V Leiden: most common inherited thrombophilia in Caucasians. Normal PT/PTT. Resistance to APC. DVT/PE/miscarriages. Worsened by OCP.
  • vWD: mucosal bleeding (NOT hemarthroses). ↑bleeding time + ↓ristocetin cofactor activity. DDAVP for Type 1. Most common inherited bleeding disorder overall.
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome: paradox — ↑PTT in vitro but HYPERCOAGULABLE in vivo. Recurrent thrombosis + miscarriages. Treat with lifelong anticoagulation.
