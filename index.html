Definition & Pathophysiology

Malignant clonal proliferation of immature myeloid precursors (blasts) arrested in early differentiation. Acquired somatic mutations → clonal expansion of myeloid stem cell → differentiation arrest → accumulation of non-functional blasts → bone marrow failure (anemia, thrombocytopenia, neutropenia). Key mutations: FLT3-ITD (internal tandem duplication — poor prognosis), NPM1 (good prognosis), CEBPA (double mutant — good prognosis), and critical subtype t(15;17) → PML-RARα fusion → differentiation arrest at promyelocyte stage = APL (M3).

Step 1 High-Yield Pearls — AML
  • Auer rods in a patient with acute leukemia = AML (myeloid lineage). No Auer rods in ALL.
  • APL (M3, t15;17) + DIC + Auer rods in bundles (faggot cells) → treat with ATRA + ATO, not 7+3.
  • FLT3-ITD = poor prognosis; NPM1 mutated / CEBPA double mutant = good prognosis.
  • Gingival hyperplasia = monocytic AML (M4 or M5 subtypes).
  • AML is TdT negative; ALL is TdT positive — this is the key immunophenotypic distinction.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Malignant clonal proliferation of immature lymphoid precursors (lymphoblasts — B or T cell) arrested in early development, replacing normal bone marrow. Chromosomal translocations and gene mutations → arrest of lymphocyte differentiation → lymphoblast accumulation → bone marrow failure. Key genetic events: t(12;21) ETV6-RUNX1 (25% of pediatric B-ALL, most common pediatric fusion, good prognosis); t(9;22) BCR-ABL (Philadelphia chromosome, 25% of adult ALL, poor prognosis); MLL rearrangements (infant ALL, poor prognosis); hyperdiploidy (>50 chromosomes, favorable in children).

Step 1 High-Yield Pearls — ALL
  • Child + bone pain + lymphadenopathy + lymphoblasts on smear = ALL. Most common pediatric cancer.
  • TdT+ confirms lymphoid immaturity — both B-ALL and T-ALL. TdT negative = AML or mature lymphoma.
  • CNS and testes are sanctuary sites — require intrathecal methotrexate prophylaxis.
  • T-ALL in an adolescent male → look for anterior mediastinal mass (risk of SVC syndrome).
  • t(9;22) in ALL = worse prognosis → add TKI (imatinib/dasatinib). t(12;21) = favorable pediatric ALL.
  • Children >85% cure; adults ~40% cure — age is a major prognostic factor.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Clonal myeloproliferative disorder of a pluripotent stem cell driven by the BCR-ABL fusion oncogene. t(9;22) → Philadelphia chromosome → BCR-ABL fusion protein → constitutively active tyrosine kinase → uncontrolled proliferation of myeloid cells with initially intact differentiation (unlike AML — cells mature, just too many of them). Disease progresses through three phases: Chronic (years, manageable) → Accelerated (months) → Blast crisis (resembles acute leukemia, poor prognosis, myeloid 70% or lymphoid 30%).

Step 1 High-Yield Pearls — CML
  • t(9;22) Philadelphia chromosome → BCR-ABL constitutive tyrosine kinase → CML. Also in 25% adult ALL (worse prognosis).
  • Basophilia is the hallmark peripheral smear finding in CML.
  • LOW LAP score distinguishes CML from leukemoid reaction (HIGH LAP).
  • Imatinib (Gleevec) is the breakthrough TKI that converted CML from fatal to manageable — median survival from 3–5 years to near-normal with therapy.
  • Three phases: Chronic (years) → Accelerated → Blast crisis (acute leukemia, poor prognosis).

Definition & Pathophysiology

Monoclonal accumulation of functionally incompetent, mature-appearing B lymphocytes that aberrantly co-express CD5 (normally a T-cell marker) alongside CD19, CD20 (dim), and CD23. Defective apoptosis via BCL-2 overexpression → prolonged survival → accumulate in bone marrow, blood, nodes, spleen → marrow failure + immune dysfunction. Hypogammaglobulinemia → susceptibility to encapsulated bacteria. AIHA and ITP arise from dysfunctional autoantibody production. Considered a spectrum with small lymphocytic lymphoma (SLL — same biology, lymph node predominant).

Step 1 High-Yield Pearls — CLL
  • Smudge (basket) cells on peripheral smear = CLL. Most commonly seen in an elderly patient with incidental lymphocytosis.
  • Immunophenotype: CD5+ / CD19+ / CD23+ — the co-expression of CD5 on B cells is the key hallmark.
  • Watch and wait for early-stage CLL — treatment does not improve survival until symptomatic.
  • Richter transformation: sudden worsening + B symptoms + rising LDH in CLL patient = transformation to DLBCL (~5%). Poor prognosis.
  • Hypogammaglobulinemia → recurrent infections with encapsulated bacteria (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae).

Definition & Pathophysiology

Malignant monoclonal proliferation of plasma cells in bone marrow, secreting a monoclonal immunoglobulin (M protein) causing end-organ damage. Spectrum: MGUS → Smoldering MM → Symptomatic MM. Malignant plasma cells: (1) secrete M protein (IgG 55%, IgA 25%) + excess free light chains (Bence Jones proteins — nephrotoxic); (2) stimulate osteoclasts via RANKL/IL-6 and suppress osteoblasts via DKK-1 → lytic bone lesions without reactive new bone; (3) crowd normal marrow → cytopenias; (4) suppress normal Ig production → immunodeficiency.

Step 1 High-Yield Pearls — Multiple Myeloma
  • Elderly + bone pain + normocytic anemia + hypercalcemia + renal failure + rouleaux on smear = MM until proven otherwise.
  • CRAB criteria: Calcium ↑, Renal failure, Anemia, Bone lesions (lytic, punched-out) — defines symptomatic MM.
  • Punched-out lytic lesions have NO surrounding sclerosis — unlike metastatic cancer which often has mixed lytic/blastic lesions.
  • Bence Jones proteins precipitate at 40–60°C and redissolve at 100°C (classic heat test).
  • Plasma cell morphology: eccentric nucleus + clock-face (cartwheel) chromatin + perinuclear hof = myeloma cell.
  • MGUS → 1% annual risk of progression to myeloma. No CRAB criteria in MGUS = observe only.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Malignant lymphoma defined by Reed-Sternberg (RS) cells — large binucleated cells with prominent "owl-eye" eosinophilic nucleoli — within a reactive inflammatory background. RS cells are derived from germinal center B cells but have lost normal B-cell gene expression. They express CD30 and CD15 but lack CD45 (LCA) and usually CD20. EBV (LMP-1 oncoprotein) is associated especially with mixed cellularity. RS cells are a minority — the surrounding reactive microenvironment (lymphocytes, eosinophils, plasma cells, macrophages) drives symptoms via cytokine release (IL-5 → eosinophilia; IL-6 → fever; IL-13 → pruritus). Spreads contiguously node-to-node in an orderly, predictable fashion.

Lymphocyte-depleted

Step 1 High-Yield Pearls — Hodgkin Lymphoma
  • Reed-Sternberg cells: CD30+ / CD15+ / CD45− — large binucleated with "owl-eye" nucleoli. Hallmark of HL.
  • HL spreads contiguously (node-to-node, orderly). NHL spreads non-contiguously (skips nodes, disseminated).
  • Alcohol-induced pain at lymph node sites is pathognomonic of Hodgkin lymphoma.
  • Nodular sclerosis = most common subtype; young woman + mediastinal mass + collagen bands + lacunar cells.
  • Pel-Ebstein fever = cyclical fever pattern, classic but rare in HL.
  • ABVD is standard regimen. Bleomycin → pulmonary toxicity (monitor PFTs). Adriamycin → cardiotoxicity.

Definition & Overview

A diverse group of lymphoid malignancies of B cells (85%) or T cells (15%), unified by the absence of Reed-Sternberg cells and non-contiguous, unpredictable spread. Classified by cell of origin, morphology, grade (indolent vs aggressive), and molecular/cytogenetic features. Far more common extranodal involvement than HL. GI tract is the most common extranodal site. Waldeyer ring involvement (tonsils, pharynx) is characteristic of NHL over HL.

High-Yield Subtypes
DLBCL — most common adult NHL
Aggressive; de novo or Richter transformation. Genetics: BCL6; MYC + BCL2 = "double-hit" = very poor. Treatment: R-CHOP × 6 cycles. CAR-T for relapsed.
Follicular lymphoma
Second most common. Indolent; waxing/waning nodes. t(14;18) → BCL2 overexpression → anti-apoptosis. Can transform to DLBCL. Watch-and-wait if asymptomatic; rituximab ± chemo when needed.
Burkitt lymphoma — high-yield
t(8;14) → MYC overexpression → Ki-67 ~100%. Endemic (Africa, jaw, EBV), sporadic (abdomen/ileocecal), immunodeficiency-related (HIV). "Starry sky" histology. Intensive chemo; tumor lysis prophylaxis mandatory.
Mantle cell lymphoma
t(11;14) → Cyclin D1 overexpression → uncontrolled G1→S. CD5+; moderately aggressive; disseminated at diagnosis; GI polyposis. Poor prognosis.
MALT lymphoma
Gastric MALT: H. pylori chronic stimulation → B-cell malignancy. Early-stage: H. pylori eradication alone induces remission. Marginal zone origin.
Primary CNS lymphoma
Almost always DLBCL. Strongly associated with HIV/immunosuppression. Periventricular lesions on MRI. Treat with high-dose methotrexate (not WBRT as first-line).

Step 1 High-Yield Pearls — Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma
  • t(14;18) = Follicular (BCL2 anti-apoptosis). t(8;14) = Burkitt (MYC, 100% Ki-67). t(11;14) = Mantle cell (Cyclin D1).
  • Starry sky pattern = Burkitt lymphoma — tingible-body macrophages engulfing apoptotic tumor cells.
  • R-CHOP = DLBCL treatment. Rituximab is anti-CD20; works for all B-cell lymphomas expressing CD20.
  • NHL does NOT spread contiguously — unlike HL which spreads in an orderly node-to-node fashion.
  • GI tract is most common extranodal NHL site. MALT lymphoma (gastric) = H. pylori → treat the bacteria, cure the lymphoma (early stage).
  • Tumor lysis syndrome = emergency with Burkitt — give allopurinol/rasburicase prophylactically; monitor K+, phosphate, uric acid, creatinine.

Step 1 High-Yield Pearls — Breast Cancer
  • BRCA1 → TNBC (triple-negative) + ovarian cancer. BRCA2 → ER+ tumors + male breast cancer + pancreatic cancer.
  • Tamoxifen (premenopausal): SERM — agonist in bone/uterus, antagonist in breast. AE: endometrial cancer + DVT.
  • Trastuzumab (HER2+): cardiomyopathy risk — check LVEF before starting and during therapy.
  • Peau d'orange + warm, erythematous breast without discrete mass = inflammatory breast cancer (dermal lymphatic obstruction).
  • Spiculated irregular mass with microcalcifications on mammogram = malignant until proven otherwise.
  • Lumpectomy + radiation = mastectomy in overall survival — equivalent for early-stage tumors.
  • Li-Fraumeni syndrome (TP53 germline): Sarcoma, Breast, brain, Leukemia, Adrenal — SBLA mnemonic.

Step 1 High-Yield Pearls — Cervical Cancer
  • HPV 16 → squamous cell carcinoma (80%). HPV 18 → adenocarcinoma. Both: E6 destroys p53; E7 destroys Rb.
  • Koilocytes (perinuclear halo + nuclear atypia) on Pap smear = HPV cytopathic effect — pathognomonic.
  • Pap smear every 3 years (ages 21–29); every 5 years with co-testing (ages 30–65).
  • Begin screening at age 21 regardless of sexual debut or HPV vaccination status.
  • DES in utero → clear cell adenocarcinoma of vagina and cervix in the daughters — rare but classic board question.
  • Postcoital bleeding is the classic early warning sign of invasive cervical cancer.
  • Obstructive uropathy (hydronephrosis) is the most common cause of death in advanced cervical cancer.
  • Gardasil 9 vaccine: 9 HPV types (6, 11, 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, 58). Prevents ~90% of cervical cancers.

Definition & Classification Framework

Anemia is a reduction in hemoglobin concentration (WHO: Hgb <12 g/dL in women, <13 g/dL in men) leading to decreased oxygen delivery. The diagnostic approach uses two axes: MCV (cell size) and reticulocyte count (bone marrow response). Step 1: Is the reticulocyte count high (destruction/loss) or low (production failure)? Step 2: Use MCV to classify production failures into micro-, normo-, or macrocytic subtypes.

Microcytic (MCV <80 fL)

Mnemonic: TAILS
Thalassemia · Anemia of chronic disease · Iron deficiency · Lead poisoning · Sideroblastic anemia


Mechanism: deficient heme or globin synthesis → small, pale RBCs

Normocytic (MCV 80–100 fL)

Causes: Acute blood loss, hemolytic anemias, anemia of chronic disease (early), aplastic anemia, renal failure (↓EPO), hypothyroidism, bone marrow infiltration


Key: check reticulocyte count to separate high (hemolysis/loss) from low (production failure)

Macrocytic (MCV >100 fL)

Megaloblastic: B12 deficiency, folate deficiency (DNA synthesis defect)
Non-megaloblastic: alcohol, liver disease, hypothyroidism, MDS, reticulocytosis, drugs (MTX, hydroxyurea)

Reticulocyte Count — The Key Branch Point
High reticulocytes (>2–3%) = Appropriate BM response
Bone marrow is working fine but RBCs are being lost or destroyed. Think: hemorrhage (acute blood loss) or hemolysis (intrinsic or extrinsic RBC destruction).
Low reticulocytes (<2%) = Inadequate BM response
Bone marrow is failing to produce enough RBCs. Classify by MCV → microcytic (TAILS), normocytic (aplastic, renal failure), or macrocytic (B12/folate, liver, alcohol).

Step 1 Pearls — Anemia General
  • First branch point: Is the reticulocyte count high (loss/destruction) or low (production failure)?
  • Second branch point (if low retics): What is the MCV? Microcytic → TAILS; Normocytic → aplastic/renal/early IDA; Macrocytic → B12/folate/alcohol/liver.
  • Hemolysis findings: high retics + ↑indirect bilirubin + ↑LDH + ↓haptoglobin.
  • RDW ↑ in iron deficiency and B12/folate deficiency (anisocytosis); RDW normal in thalassemia trait (uniform small cells).
  • Early iron deficiency can be normocytic — MCV drops only after prolonged depletion.

Step 1 Pearls — Microcytic Anemias
  • IDA: ferritin↓ (first to deplete) + TIBC↑ + serum iron↓. Ferritin is the most sensitive early marker.
  • ACD: serum iron↓ but ferritin↑ + TIBC↓ — iron is trapped in macrophages by hepcidin. The key distinguisher from IDA.
  • Sideroblastic anemia: serum iron↑ + ferritin↑ + ringed sideroblasts on bone marrow. INH → give B6. Lead → give DMSA/EDTA.
  • Thalassemia trait: microcytic anemia with NORMAL iron studies + ELEVATED RBC count + target cells on smear. Confirm with Hgb electrophoresis.
  • Basophilic stippling with ringed sideroblasts = sideroblastic anemia. Basophilic stippling WITHOUT ringed sideroblasts = lead poisoning.
  • Pica + koilonychia + smooth tongue + esophageal webs (Plummer-Vinson) = Iron deficiency anemia.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Megaloblastic anemia = defect in DNA synthesis → delayed nuclear maturation relative to cytoplasmic maturation → large cells with immature nuclei (megaloblasts in marrow; macro-ovalocytes and hypersegmented neutrophils in blood). Caused by deficiency of Vitamin B12 or Folate — both required for thymidine synthesis (via methylation pathways). Non-megaloblastic macrocytosis (no DNA synthesis defect): alcohol, liver disease, hypothyroidism, reticulocytosis, MDS, drugs.

Step 1 Pearls — Megaloblastic Anemia
  • Hypersegmented neutrophils (>5 lobes) on smear = megaloblastic anemia; earliest and most specific finding.
  • ↑MMA + ↑homocysteine = B12 deficiency. ↑Homocysteine only (normal MMA) = folate deficiency.
  • Neurologic symptoms (subacute combined degeneration) = B12 deficiency ONLY. Folate deficiency has NO neuro involvement.
  • Pernicious anemia: autoimmune → anti-parietal cell Ab + anti-intrinsic factor Ab → no B12 absorption → IM B12 replacement.
  • Methotrexate and trimethoprim inhibit dihydrofolate reductase → folate deficiency → macrocytic anemia.
  • Crohn's disease affecting terminal ileum → B12 malabsorption (terminal ileum is the only absorption site for B12-IF complex).

Definition & Pathophysiology

Thalassemias are inherited autosomal recessive disorders of globin chain synthesis. Reduced production of one chain type → relative excess of the other → excess chains precipitate → damage RBC membrane → hemolysis and ineffective erythropoiesis. Iron studies are normal (iron absorption intact), which is the key distinguisher from iron deficiency anemia. Compensatory: ↑HbA2 and HbF in beta-thalassemia; bone marrow expands; extramedullary hematopoiesis.

Step 1 Pearls — Thalassemia
  • Thalassemia trait: microcytic anemia + NORMAL iron studies + ELEVATED RBC count + target cells. Confirm with Hgb electrophoresis.
  • Beta-thal major: presents 6–12 months when HbF fades. Frontal bossing + crew-cut skull X-ray (marrow expansion) + hepatosplenomegaly + transfusion-dependent.
  • Alpha-thal: 4 genes, 4 presentations. 4 deleted = hydrops fetalis (incompatible with life). Normal electrophoresis in trait (2 genes deleted).
  • Do NOT give iron to thalassemia trait — iron studies are normal. Iron supplementation causes iron overload.
  • Target cells mnemonic HALT: HbC disease, Asplenia, Liver disease, Thalassemia.
  • Iron overload from chronic transfusions: chelation with deferoxamine or deferasirox prevents cardiomyopathy and cirrhosis.
