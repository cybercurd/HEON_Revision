Definition & Pathophysiology

A clinical syndrome of pain radiating along the sciatic nerve distribution (buttock → posterior thigh → leg), most often caused by lumbosacral nerve root irritation rather than being a distinct diagnosis itself. Most commonly affected intervertebral discs are L4–L5 and L5–S1. The sciatic nerve (L4-S3) is often compressed by disc herniation, posterior hip dislocation, or piriformis syndrome — injury causes radiating posterior leg pain, a positive straight leg raise, and common peroneal/tibial neuropathy. Age-related degenerative changes including facet joint hypertrophy, ligamentum flavum thickening, and spondylolisthesis (causing spinal stenosis) may also cause sciatica, typically presenting as neurogenic claudication rather than classic radicular pain.

Cauda equina syndromeRare but devastating complication — impaired bladder, bowel, and genital dysfunction, saddle anesthesia, bilateral leg weakness — surgical emergency

Summary Pearls — Sciatica
  • Positive SLR test reproducing pain between 30-70° of leg elevation is classic for nerve root tension from disc herniation.
  • L4-L5 and L5-S1 discs account for the vast majority of sciatica cases (>90% involve L5/S1 roots).
  • Cauda equina syndrome (saddle anesthesia, bowel/bladder dysfunction, bilateral leg weakness) is a surgical emergency — do not delay imaging or surgical consultation.
  • Piriformis syndrome is a non-disc cause of sciatica — the piriformis muscle compresses the sciatic nerve as it exits the pelvis.
  • Neurogenic claudication (from spinal stenosis) improves with sitting/flexion — distinguishes it from vascular claudication, which improves with rest regardless of position.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Degenerative disc disease refers to displacement of disc material into the spinal canal — protrusion, herniation, and sequestration — resulting in mechanical compression of either the spinal cord or a nerve root. The annulus fibrosus weakens with age and repetitive mechanical stress, allowing the gel-like nucleus pulposus to bulge or extrude posteriorly or posterolaterally (the path of least resistance, since the posterior longitudinal ligament is thinner laterally), directly compressing the adjacent nerve root.

Clinical Features
Classic triadRadicular pain in the dermatome of the compressed nerve root, muscle weakness, and loss of deep tendon reflexes in the indicator muscles
LocalizationThe location of the lesion can often be determined using the patient's neurological deficit pattern
L5 compression (most common)Reduced sensitivity in the lateral leg and dorsum of the foot, with weakness in extending the big toe (EHL)
OnsetOften acute, frequently following heavy lifting, twisting, or axial load; may also be gradual/degenerative
Key Distinguishing Features
Bragard signRaising the straight leg, then dorsiflexing the foot at the point of pain onset, increases pain further if a true nerve root tension sign is present — helps differentiate true radicular pain from simple hamstring tightness
Exiting vs traversing rootA paracentral (posterolateral) herniation at one level typically compresses the TRAVERSING root of the level below, not the exiting root at that level
EMG findingsDenervation pattern in muscles innervated by the affected nerve root confirms nerve compression and helps exclude peripheral neuropathy

Summary Pearls — Disc Herniation
  • L4-L5 and L5-S1 are by far the most commonly herniated discs due to high mechanical stress at the lumbosacral junction.
  • Exiting vs traversing root rule: a paracentral L4-L5 herniation typically compresses the traversing L5 root, not the exiting L4 root.
  • L5 root compression: weak big toe extension (EHL), lateral leg/dorsal foot sensory loss, NO reliable reflex change (L5 has no major deep tendon reflex).
  • Bragard sign (SLR + ankle dorsiflexion increasing pain) confirms a true neural tension sign over simple muscular tightness.
  • Most disc herniations resolve spontaneously with conservative management within 6-12 weeks — surgery is reserved for progressive deficits or failure of conservative therapy.

Definition & Pathophysiology

A general term for dysfunction of a single spinal nerve root, causing pain, sensory changes, weakness, and/or reflex changes in the corresponding dermatomal/myotomal distribution. (Sciatica is technically a form of lumbosacral radiculopathy.) Compression, inflammation, or irritation of a nerve root as it exits the spinal canal — most commonly from disc herniation, but also from spinal stenosis, spondylolisthesis, osteophyte formation, or less commonly tumor/infection.

Summary Pearls — Radiculopathy
  • Memorize the C5-C7 and L4-S1 motor/reflex/sensory table above — this is one of the highest-yield localization tables on Summary.
  • L5 has NO reliable reflex — a patient with normal reflexes but foot drop and weak big toe extension still localizes to L5.
  • S1 radiculopathy = weak plantarflexion + ↓Achilles reflex + lateral foot/sole sensory loss.
  • Dermatomal (radiculopathy) vs stocking-glove (peripheral neuropathy) vs multi-root non-dermatomal (plexopathy) — this distinction is frequently tested.

Definition & Pathophysiology

A chronic inflammatory autoimmune disease that mainly affects the spine joints, causing severe chronic pain and, in advanced cases, spine fusion — the prototypical seronegative spondyloarthropathy. Immune cells and innate cytokines are crucial to pathogenesis, especially HLA-B27 and the interleukin-23/17 axis. In advanced cases, inflammation leads to fibrosis and calcification, resulting in loss of flexibility and fusion of the spine, resembling "bamboo" in an immobile position. There is a correlation between AS prevalence and HLA-B27 — among HLA-B27 positive individuals, AS prevalence is approximately 5-6%.

Summary Pearls — Ankylosing Spondylitis
  • Young man + morning back stiffness that IMPROVES with exercise (inflammatory pattern) + HLA-B27 positive + decreased lumbar flexion = ankylosing spondylitis.
  • Bamboo spine = late radiographic finding from syndesmophyte-mediated vertebral fusion. Sacroiliitis is the earliest finding.
  • The "A" extra-articular associations: Anterior uveitis, Aortic regurgitation, AV block, Apical fibrosis, Amyloidosis, Achilles enthesitis.
  • Inflammatory back pain (worse with rest, better with activity) is the opposite pattern of mechanical/degenerative back pain (worse with activity, better with rest) — a key Summary discriminator.
  • TNF-alpha inhibitors are highly effective for axial disease, unlike traditional DMARDs (methotrexate), which work poorly for the spine in AS.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Complete loss of congruity between two articulating bone surfaces at a joint (as opposed to subluxation, which is partial displacement). Most commonly from traumatic forces exceeding the stabilizing capacity of the joint capsule, ligaments, and surrounding musculature. The shoulder (glenohumeral joint) is the most commonly dislocated major joint in the body due to its shallow glenoid fossa and wide range of motion. Lunate dislocation typically occurs from a fall on an outstretched hand and can compress the median nerve, leading to acute carpal tunnel syndrome.

Posterior shoulder

Posterior hip (~90%)
Leg shortened, flexed, adducted, internally rotated. Classic "dashboard injury." Risk: sciatic nerve
Anterior hip
Leg abducted and externally rotated. Risk: femoral neurovascular bundle
Knee (tibiofemoral)

⚠ Knee dislocation, even after spontaneous reduction, mandates evaluation for popliteal artery injury — this is limb-threatening if missed and is a classic "don't miss" teaching point.
Summary Pearls — Dislocations
  • Posterior hip dislocation (leg flexed, adducted, internally rotated) is THE classic dashboard injury and threatens the sciatic nerve.
  • Anterior shoulder dislocation (>95% of shoulder dislocations) risks the axillary nerve — test sensation over the deltoid "regimental badge" area.
  • Posterior shoulder dislocation is classically associated with seizures or electrical shock.
  • Knee dislocation = mandatory vascular workup for popliteal artery injury, regardless of whether it has spontaneously reduced.
  • Lunate dislocation from FOOSH can cause acute median nerve compression (acute carpal tunnel syndrome).

Definition & Healing Stages

A break in the structural continuity of bone, classified by pattern (transverse, oblique, spiral, comminuted), displacement, and whether open (compound) or closed. Healing proceeds through four overlapping stages: (1) Hematoma formation (days 1-5) — fracture hematoma provides initial scaffold and cytokine signaling; (2) Soft callus formation via fibrocartilage (weeks 2-3); (3) Hard (bony) callus formation via endochondral ossification (weeks 3-6); (4) Remodeling (months to years), restoring normal architecture along lines of mechanical stress per Wolff's law.

Summary Pearls — Fractures
  • Salter-Harris Type II is the most common pediatric growth plate fracture overall.
  • Higher Salter-Harris numbers (III, IV, V) carry progressively worse prognosis for growth disturbance because they increasingly involve the germinal layer of the physis.
  • SALTR mnemonic: Straight across (I), Above (II), Lower/beLow (III), Through (IV), cRush/ERasure (V).
  • Scaphoid fractures need follow-up imaging even with an initially normal X-ray due to risk of occult fracture and avascular necrosis.
  • Supracondylar humerus fracture + absent radial pulse + cold pale hand = brachial artery injury — vascular emergency in children.

Definition & Pathophysiology — The Unhappy Triad

Disruption of knee stabilizing structures, often occurring together due to their anatomic relationship. The classic "unhappy triad" results from a lateral force to a planted foot and comprises injury to the anterior cruciate ligament (ACL), the medial collateral ligament (MCL), and the medial meniscus, since the medial meniscus is attached to the MCL. Contemporary data note that the lateral meniscus can be involved as well. This classically presents with acute pain, swelling, and instability of the knee.

Clinical Features & Key Tests
ACL tearLachman test shows increased anterior translation of the tibia — more sensitive than the anterior drawer test. Often an audible "pop" with rapid-onset hemarthrosis (within hours)
MCL tearValgus stress test reproduces pain/laxity at the medial joint line; typically from a direct lateral blow to the knee
Medial meniscus tearJoint line tenderness; McMurray test (pain/click with knee flexion + rotation); locking or catching sensation from a displaced meniscal fragment; presents with acute pain, swelling, and instability
MechanismClassic football/skiing injury — lateral force applied to a planted, weight-bearing leg
Diagnostic Workup
Plain radiographsFirst-line — rule out fracture
MRI kneeGold standard for soft tissue knee injuries — visualizes ACL, MCL, and meniscal pathology with high sensitivity/specificity
Physical exam testsLachman (ACL), valgus stress (MCL), McMurray (meniscus) — all should be performed as part of the standard knee exam
Treatment
MCL tearsTypically managed conservatively (bracing, physical therapy) given good intrinsic healing capacity from robust blood supply
ACL tearsActive/young patients usually require surgical reconstruction (autograft or allograft) due to poor intrinsic healing of the avascular ligament — primary repair is not standard
Meniscus tears (peripheral)Peripheral, vascularized "red zone" tears may be surgically repaired
Meniscus tears (central)Central, avascular "white zone" tears often require partial meniscectomy rather than repair, given poor healing potential
Summary Pearls — Ligament Tears
  • Unhappy triad = ACL + MCL + medial meniscus, from a lateral blow to a planted leg (classic football/skiing injury).
  • Lachman test > anterior drawer test for ACL sensitivity — Lachman is performed at 20-30° of knee flexion vs 90° for the drawer test.
  • The ACL has poor intrinsic healing capacity due to limited blood supply — this is why surgical reconstruction (not primary repair) is the standard of care.
  • MCL, by contrast, has a robust blood supply and heals well conservatively in most cases.
  • Red zone (peripheral, vascularized) meniscal tears can be repaired; white zone (central, avascular) tears typically require meniscectomy.

Definition & Pathophysiology

A diagnosis based on prior history of a "fragility" fracture or low bone mass density, defined as a bone densitometry T-score less than -2.5 — a systemic skeletal disease of reduced bone mass and microarchitectural deterioration leading to fragility. Pathophysiology: imbalance favoring osteoclastic bone resorption over osteoblastic bone formation. Patients typically have NORMAL serum levels of calcium, phosphorus, alkaline phosphatase, and PTH; histologic bone sections generally show thin trabeculae with normal calcification and normal amounts of osteoblasts and osteoclasts — this is purely a quantitative bone density problem, distinguishing it from metabolic bone diseases. Primary osteoporosis is the most common type, generally occurring in postmenopausal women secondary to low estrogen levels; secondary osteoporosis results from other conditions including glucocorticoid use, hyperthyroidism, and hypogonadism.

Summary Pearls — Osteoporosis
  • Postmenopausal woman + fragility fracture + normal Ca/Phos/PTH/ALP + T-score ≤-2.5 = osteoporosis.
  • Glucocorticoid use is the most common cause of secondary osteoporosis — always screen patients on chronic steroids.
  • Normal labs are the key distinguishing feature from osteomalacia and hyperparathyroidism — osteoporosis is a quantity problem, not a quality/mineralization problem.
  • Bisphosphonates are first-line — remember the classic side effect of osteonecrosis of the jaw and atypical femoral fractures with long-term use.
  • Teriparatide (PTH analog) is unique among osteoporosis drugs in being anabolic (builds new bone) rather than purely antiresorptive.

Definition & Pathophysiology

An increase in parathyroid hormone (PTH) levels in the blood, classified as primary (autonomous PTH overproduction) or secondary (appropriate PTH elevation in response to hypocalcemia). Primary causes: parathyroid adenoma (most common), multiple benign tumors (hyperplasia), or parathyroid cancer (rare). Secondary causes: vitamin D deficiency, chronic kidney disease, or low blood calcium. PTH stimulates osteoclasts by binding to its receptor on osteoblasts, inducing RANK-L and M-CSF synthesis; it also increases reabsorption of Ca2+ in the distal convoluted tubule and stimulates kidney 1α-hydroxylase in the proximal convoluted tubule to increase calcitriol production.

"Bones, stones, groans, and psychiatric overtones" — the classic mnemonic for symptomatic hypercalcemia from hyperparathyroidism.

Summary Pearls — Hyperparathyroidism
  • High Ca + high PTH + low phosphate = primary hyperparathyroidism (adenoma most common cause).
  • High Ca + LOW/suppressed PTH = hypercalcemia of malignancy (PTHrP-mediated) — NOT hyperparathyroidism.
  • Low Ca + high PTH + high phosphate = secondary hyperparathyroidism, classically from CKD.
  • 24-hour urine calcium distinguishes primary HPTH (high) from familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia (low) — a classic trick diagnosis.
  • "Bones, stones, groans, psychiatric overtones" captures the classic symptomatic presentation.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Deficient PTH secretion or action, resulting in hypocalcemia and hyperphosphatemia — essentially the inverse lab pattern of primary hyperparathyroidism. Most often the result of surgical removal or damage to the parathyroid glands (e.g., inadvertent removal during thyroidectomy). Other causes include autoimmune destruction (autoimmune polyglandular syndrome type 1), congenital lack of functioning parathyroid glands (DiGeorge syndrome — 22q11 deletion), and very low magnesium levels, which impairs PTH secretion. Low PTH leads to low calcium and high phosphate levels.

Clinical Features
Neuromuscular irritabilityFrom hypocalcemia — perioral numbness/tingling, muscle cramps, tetany
Severe casesSeizures; laryngospasm; prolonged QT interval on ECG (risk of arrhythmia/torsades)
Chronic findingsDry skin, brittle nails, cataracts (chronic hypocalcemia), dental abnormalities if onset in childhood
DiGeorge syndromeCongenital absence of parathyroid (and thymus) development — presents in infancy with hypocalcemic seizures, plus T-cell immunodeficiency and cardiac defects
Key Distinguishing Features
Chvostek signFacial muscle twitching upon tapping the facial nerve (anterior to the ear) — classic tetany sign
Trousseau signCarpal spasm upon inflating a blood pressure cuff above systolic pressure for several minutes — more specific than Chvostek
PseudohypoparathyroidismA related disorder — patients present with signs/symptoms of hypoparathyroidism but have NORMAL or ELEVATED PTH levels, because these patients have PTH hormone RESISTANCE in target organs (not a deficiency of PTH itself)
Diagnostic Workup
Serum calciumLow (ionized calcium most accurate)
Serum phosphateHigh — distinguishes from vitamin D deficiency, where phosphate is typically low/normal
Serum PTHLow in true hypoparathyroidism; normal/high in pseudohypoparathyroidism (resistance, not deficiency)
Serum magnesiumCritical to check — rule out hypomagnesemia as a reversible cause of functional hypoparathyroidism
Treatment
Acute/symptomaticIV calcium gluconate for severe symptomatic hypocalcemia (tetany, seizures, arrhythmia)
MaintenanceOral calcium and active vitamin D (calcitriol) supplementation
Magnesium repletionEssential if hypomagnesemic — PTH secretion will NOT normalize until magnesium is corrected, even with calcium replacement
Refractory casesRecombinant PTH (teriparatide-like agents) for select refractory patients
Always check magnesium in hypocalcemia. Severe hypomagnesemia suppresses PTH secretion (both reduces release and causes end-organ resistance), causing a "functional" hypoparathyroidism that will NOT correct with calcium supplementation alone — magnesium must be repleted first.
Summary Pearls — Hypoparathyroidism
  • Post-thyroidectomy patient with perioral tingling and positive Chvostek/Trousseau sign = hypoparathyroidism from inadvertent parathyroid gland removal/devascularization.
  • Lab pattern: ↓Calcium + ↑Phosphate + ↓PTH = true hypoparathyroidism.
  • Pseudohypoparathyroidism: same symptoms, but PTH is normal/HIGH — the defect is target organ resistance, not hormone deficiency (often with short stature, short 4th/5th metacarpals — Albright hereditary osteodystrophy).
  • Always rule out hypomagnesemia in hypocalcemia — it suppresses PTH and won't respond to calcium alone.
  • DiGeorge syndrome = congenital hypoparathyroidism + T-cell deficiency + cardiac defects (22q11 deletion) — presents with neonatal hypocalcemic seizures.

Definition & Pathophysiology

A common, non-inflammatory musculoskeletal disorder resulting from the gradual loss of articular cartilage that normally cushions the ends of bones within synovial joints. Cartilage degradation leads to increased friction, joint space narrowing, and subsequent subchondral bone remodeling; the disease predominantly affects the hands, knees, hips, and spine, where mechanical stress is greatest. Over time, osteophyte formation and synovial inflammation contribute to joint stiffness and pain. Unlike inflammatory arthritis, OA features minimal synovial inflammation — it is fundamentally a mechanical/degenerative process, not autoimmune.

Summary Pearls — Osteoarthritis

  • Gelling phenomenon: brief stiffness after a period of rest, distinct from the prolonged morning stiffness of inflammatory arthritis.
  • No specific lab markers are elevated in OA — normal ESR/CRP/RF is itself a useful distinguishing feature in a joint pain vignette.
  • Subchondral tophi/punched-out lesions = gouty arthritis, NOT osteoarthritis — don't confuse the radiographic patterns.

Definition & Pathophysiology

An autoimmune disease with symmetrical joint involvement, typically occurring in young to middle-aged adults, characterized by chronic synovial inflammation leading to progressive joint destruction. Autoimmune-mediated synovitis driven by autoantibodies (rheumatoid factor, anti-CCP) and immune complex deposition, leading to pannus formation — proliferative, invasive synovial tissue — that erodes cartilage and bone over time.

Summary Pearls — Rheumatoid Arthritis
  • RA = symmetric, small joints (PIP/MCP, sparing DIP) + morning stiffness >1 hour improving with activity + RF/anti-CCP positive + elevated ESR/CRP + normocytic anemia of chronic disease.
  • Methotrexate is the first-line DMARD — start early to prevent irreversible joint damage ("window of opportunity" concept).
  • Anti-CCP is more specific than RF for RA diagnosis and may predict more erosive disease.
  • NSAIDs and glucocorticoids control symptoms but do NOT alter the disease course — DMARDs are required to prevent structural damage.
  • RA affects multiple organ systems beyond joints — pulmonary, cardiac, and cutaneous manifestations are tested concepts (rheumatoid nodules, interstitial lung disease, pericarditis).

Definition & Pathophysiology

Erb's palsy (upper trunk, C5-C6) results from injury to the C5-C6 nerve roots or the superior trunk, commonly caused by forced lateral neck extension — shoulder dystocia during childbirth, forceps delivery, or motorcycle accidents are prominent causes. Upper plexus (Erb's palsy) involvement is the most common pattern overall. Klumpke's palsy (lower trunk, C8-T1) stems from injury to the C8-T1 nerve roots or inferior trunk, typically due to forced arm abduction (e.g., grabbing something overhead during a fall, or excessive traction on an abducted arm during difficult delivery).

Summary Pearls — Brachial Plexus Injuries
  • Erb's palsy = C5-C6 = "waiter's tip" (arm adducted, internally rotated, extended elbow, flexed wrist) = far more common = better prognosis.
  • Klumpke's palsy = C8-T1 = "claw hand" = rarer = worse prognosis, often with associated Horner syndrome if sympathetic fibers are involved.
  • Erb's mechanism: forced lateral neck extension (shoulder dystocia, forceps delivery). Klumpke's mechanism: forced arm abduction (grabbing something while falling).
  • Neuropraxia (best prognosis, axon spared) → Axonotmesis (axon damaged, Wallerian degeneration) → Neurotmesis (worst, complete nerve disruption, needs surgery) — know this severity spectrum.
  • Pancoast tumor can mimic/cause lower brachial plexus symptoms — think of this in an older smoker with arm pain plus Horner syndrome.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Injury to the L1-L4 nerve network supplying the anterior thigh, medial thigh/leg, and parts of the pelvis/hip. Injury occurs in contexts like pelvic surgery, tumors, or hip dislocation. Motor effects classically involve the thigh adductors (adductor longus, brevis, magnus, gracilis) via the obturator nerve, and the iliopsoas/quadriceps via the femoral nerve.

Summary Pearls — Lumbar Plexus Injuries
  • Femoral nerve = knee extension + patellar reflex + anterior thigh sensation — classic culprit is retroperitoneal/psoas hematoma in an anticoagulated patient.

  • Always consider retroperitoneal hemorrhage in an anticoagulated patient presenting with new femoral neuropathy and groin/flank pain.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Injury to the L4-S4 nerve network, principally giving rise to the sciatic nerve and its branches, supplying the posterior thigh, leg, and foot. The sciatic nerve (L4-S3) is often compressed by disc herniation, posterior hip dislocation, or piriformis syndrome — injury causes radiating posterior leg pain, a positive straight leg raise, and common peroneal/tibial neuropathy. The sciatic nerve splits into the common peroneal and tibial nerves and supplies all muscles below the knee.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Entrapment of the median nerve as it passes under the transverse carpal ligament, causing paresthesia, pain, and numbness in the median nerve distribution. Median nerve entrapment occurs under the transverse carpal ligament, along with the flexor digitorum superficialis and profundus tendons, within the confined space of the carpal tunnel. Anything that increases pressure within this compartment — edema, tenosynovitis, bony abnormality — can compress the nerve.

Definition & Classification

Heart failure is a clinical syndrome resulting from complex pathophysiological processes that impair ventricular structure or function, classified according to LVEF into three categories: HFrEF (LVEF ≤40%), HFmrEF (LVEF 41-49%, "mildly reduced," with features of both HFrEF and HFpEF), and HFpEF (LVEF ≥50%). Each phenotype has distinct pathophysiological mechanisms and clinical features. Congestive heart failure (CHF) is the general clinical term used when heart failure causes fluid accumulation (congestion) in the lungs and/or peripheral tissues — it is not a separate disease, but describes the fluid-overload state common to advanced HF of any type.

HFrEF — Systolic Dysfunction
PathophysiologyImpaired myocardial contractility → ↓stroke volume → ↓cardiac output. Common etiologies: ischemic heart disease (MI), chronic hypertension, dilated cardiomyopathy
RemodelingEccentric hypertrophy — dilated chamber, thin walls. Major structural abnormality is eccentric remodeling with progressive ventricular dilatation and volume overload
AuscultationS3 gallop (early diastole) from rapid ventricular filling into a dilated, compliant ventricle
Mechanism driving progressionCardiomyocyte loss drives remodeling; LV dilation increases wall stress, worsening contractility over time (vicious cycle)
HFpEF — Diastolic Dysfunction
PathophysiologyImpaired ventricular relaxation and filling → ↑LV end-diastolic pressure (LVEDP). The ventricle can't relax, so it can't properly fill, despite preserved "squeeze" (systolic) function
RemodelingConcentric hypertrophy — thickened ventricular walls with smaller chamber size, accompanied by impaired myocardial relaxation and increased stiffness (pressure overload pattern)
AuscultationS4 gallop, representing atrial contraction into a stiff, noncompliant ventricle
MechanismUnlike HFrEF's cardiomyocyte loss, HFpEF remodeling may stem from coronary microvascular inflammation and deranged intramyocardial signaling; cardiomyocytes show increased collagen and titin contributing to stiffness. Frequently associated with obesity, diabetes, hypertension, and aging
Left-Sided vs Right-Sided HF — Clinical Features
Left-sided HFResults in pulmonary venous congestion. Symptoms: dyspnea, orthopnea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea, pulmonary edema. Signs: bibasilar crackles, S3 heart sound
Right-sided HFOften occurs secondary to left-sided HF (isolated right HF from pulmonary disease = cor pulmonale). Symptoms: peripheral edema, ascites, hepatomegaly. Signs: elevated JVP, peripheral edema, congestive hepatomegaly ("nutmeg liver")
General HF symptomsWorsening ventricular dysfunction → more severe symptoms; no single sign or symptom is pathognomonic for heart failure — diagnosis relies on the overall clinical gestalt

Key conceptNo therapies have proven mortality benefit in HFpEF (unlike HFrEF) — treatment focuses on symptom relief and controlling comorbidities

Summary high-yield distinction: HFrEF is treated with medications that improve survival (ACEi/ARB/ARNI, beta-blockers, MRAs, SGLT2i), while HFpEF management focuses primarily on symptom relief and comorbidity control, since no therapy has yet proven a mortality benefit in HFpEF.
Summary Pearls — Heart Failure
  • HFrEF = systolic/contractility problem = eccentric hypertrophy = S3 gallop = EF≤40%. HFpEF = diastolic/relaxation problem = concentric hypertrophy = S4 gallop = EF≥50%.
  • S3 = volume overload (HFrEF, regurgitant valve disease). S4 = stiff ventricle (HFpEF, LVH, ischemia) — a classic auscultation pairing.
  • Left HF → pulmonary symptoms (dyspnea, orthopnea, PND, crackles). Right HF → systemic congestion (JVD, edema, hepatomegaly/nutmeg liver, ascites).
  • Only HFrEF has proven mortality-reducing pharmacotherapy (ACEi/ARNI + beta-blocker + MRA + SGLT2i = the "four pillars"). HFpEF treatment is symptomatic and comorbidity-focused.
  • Cor pulmonale = isolated right heart failure FROM a primary pulmonary disease process (e.g., COPD), as opposed to right HF secondary to left HF.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Bradycardia (heart rate <60 bpm) results from increased vagal tone, medications (beta-blockers, non-dihydropyridine CCBs), sinus node dysfunction, or conduction system disease. Atrioventricular (AV) conduction is evaluated by assessing the relationship between P waves and QRS complexes; normally, a P wave precedes each QRS complex with a fixed PR interval of 120-200 milliseconds. AV block represents a delay or disturbance in the transmission of an impulse from the atria to the ventricles, due to anatomical or functional impairment in the heart's conduction system. Bundle branch blocks occur when one of the bundle branches (left or right) of the cardiac conduction system is interrupted, causing asynchronous ventricular depolarization.

Summary Pearls — Bradyarrhythmias & Blocks
  • First-degree AV block: PR >200ms, every beat conducts — benign, no treatment.
  • Mobitz I (Wenckebach): progressively lengthening PR → dropped beat → resets. "Longer, longer, drop, now you have a Wenckebach." Usually benign.
  • Mobitz II: fixed PR with sudden dropped beats — infranodal disease, high risk of progressing to complete block, usually needs a pacemaker.
  • Third-degree (complete) block: total AV dissociation — P waves and QRS complexes march independently at their own rates.
  • RBBB: RSR' in V1-V2 ("rabbit ears"). LBBB: broad monophasic R in V6/lead I, QS in V1 — LBBB more often signals structural heart disease.
  • Simultaneous RBBB + LBBB = complete (third-degree) AV block, since both bundle branches are non-functional.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Supraventricular tachyarrhythmias (SVTs) occur in regions of the heart other than the ventricles, originating in either the atrioventricular (AV) node or a location in the atria. Re-entry circuits are the most common underlying mechanism for most SVTs. SVTs can be less likely than VT or VF to result in patient death, but they can still cause significant patient discomfort and elevated stroke risk, particularly atrial fibrillation. Examples include atrial fibrillation (AF), atrial flutter (AFl), AV-nodal reentrant tachycardia (AVNRT), AV reciprocating tachycardia (AVRT), and multifocal atrial tachycardia (MAT).

MechanismRapid, irregular electrical activity in the atria, leading to uncoordinated atrial contraction — the inactive "worm bag-like" oscillation of the atria with absence of true atrial contraction
ECG findingsIrregularly irregular rhythm; absence of distinct P waves; "irregularly irregular" QRS complexes
Clinical significanceMost common sustained arrhythmia; significantly increases risk of embolic stroke due to blood stasis in the fibrillating left atrial appendage

Atrial Flutter

Acute hemodynamic instability from ANY tachyarrhythmia (SVT or ventricular) → immediate synchronized DC cardioversion, regardless of the specific rhythm diagnosis. Stable patients allow time for pharmacologic management and rate/rhythm control strategy selection.
Summary Pearls — Supraventricular Arrhythmias
  • AFib: irregularly irregular, no discrete P waves — most common sustained arrhythmia, highest embolic stroke risk among SVTs.
  • Atrial flutter: regular sawtooth P waves, reentrant circuit around tricuspid annulus, often 2:1 conduction (atrial rate ~300, ventricular rate ~150).
  • PSVT (AVNRT/AVRT): narrow-complex, regular, sudden onset/offset — adenosine and vagal maneuvers are first-line acute treatment.
  • MAT: ≥3 P-wave morphologies — classically seen in COPD patients; treat the underlying lung disease, not the rhythm itself.
  • CHA2DS2-VASc score guides anticoagulation decisions in both AFib and atrial flutter to prevent embolic stroke.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Ventricular arrhythmias arise from ectopic foci or reentry circuits within the ventricular myocardium itself, bypassing the normal His-Purkinje conduction sequence, which produces wide QRS complexes. They are generally more dangerous than supraventricular arrhythmias because they directly compromise coordinated ventricular contraction and cardiac output, and can rapidly degenerate into ventricular fibrillation and cardiac arrest.

Summary Pearls — Ventricular Arrhythmias
  • ≥3 consecutive PVCs = ventricular tachycardia. Wide QRS complex tachycardia should be assumed to be VT until proven otherwise, especially in patients with structural heart disease.
  • Torsades de pointes = polymorphic VT + prolonged QT. Treatment = IV magnesium, regardless of serum Mg2+ level.
  • VFib = chaotic, no organized QRS, no cardiac output = immediate unsynchronized defibrillation.
  • Defibrillation (unsynchronized) for VF/pulseless VT; synchronized cardioversion for unstable but perfusing rhythms (stable VT with a pulse, unstable AFib/flutter).
  • Acquired long QT (drugs, electrolyte abnormalities) and congenital long QT syndromes both predispose to torsades — always review the medication list and electrolytes in any patient with new ventricular arrhythmia.

Summary Pearls — Arrhythmogenic Disorders
  • Brugada: coved ST elevation in V1-V3, SCN5A loss-of-function, events at REST/fever — NOT provoked by exercise. ICD is the only proven therapy.
  • WPW: short PR + delta wave + wide QRS. NEVER give AV nodal blockers (adenosine, CCB, beta-blocker, digoxin) if WPW + AFib is suspected — can precipitate VF. Ablation is curative.
  • Romano-Ward = autosomal dominant LQTS, no deafness (most common). Jervell-Lange-Nielsen = autosomal recessive LQTS + deafness.
  • LQT1 (KCNQ1) → exercise/swimming triggers. LQT2 (KCNH2) → auditory/emotional triggers. LQT3 (SCN5A) → triggers during sleep/rest.
  • HCM is the #1 cause of sudden cardiac death in young athletes. Murmur gets LOUDER with decreased preload (Valsalva, standing) — opposite of most murmurs, due to dynamic outflow obstruction.
  • ARVC: epsilon wave, fibrofatty RV replacement, arrhythmias provoked BY exercise (contrast with Brugada) — also a major cause of athlete sudden death.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Hypertension is sustained elevation of systemic arterial blood pressure, classified as primary (essential) hypertension (~95% of cases — multifactorial, involving genetics, sympathetic overactivity, RAAS dysregulation, sodium retention, and vascular stiffness, without single identifiable cause) or secondary hypertension (~5% — identifiable underlying cause). Chronically elevated pressure damages the vascular endothelium, accelerates atherosclerosis, and causes target-organ damage through both direct mechanical stress and triggered remodeling.

  • Coarctation of the aorta: upper extremity HTN + weak/delayed femoral pulses; associated with Turner syndrome.
  • Hypertensive urgency (severe BP, no end-organ damage) vs emergency (severe BP WITH end-organ damage) — only the latter requires immediate IV therapy and ICU-level BP reduction.
  • Chronic HTN → concentric LVH → diastolic dysfunction → HFpEF; this is one of the most common causes of HFpEF on Summary.

Definitions & Pathophysiology

Aortic dissection occurs when a tear in the inner layer (intima) of the aorta allows blood to flow between the layers of the aortic wall (intima and media), creating a false lumen — blood enters through the tear and splits the layers, which can compromise blood flow to major branch vessels or cause rupture. Aortic aneurysm is abnormal focal dilation of the aorta, classified as thoracic (TAA) or abdominal (AAA) — AAA is most common and usually occurs below the renal arteries. Vessel wall weakening in both conditions results from loss of smooth muscle cells, elastic fibers, and collagen — chronic inflammation and degradation of extracellular matrix proteins play a central role, especially in AAAs.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Peripheral artery disease (PAD), also referred to more broadly as peripheral vascular disease (PVD), is most commonly caused by atherosclerosis — the obstruction of large peripheral arteries leading to narrowing (stenosis) or obstruction by thrombus or embolism, resulting in ischemia that can be acute (rapid onset, short duration) or chronic (long-term). Atherosclerotic disease leads to progressive obstruction of the peripheral arteries → lack of blood flow → ischemia and pain, most commonly affecting the lower extremities, with the popliteal artery being the most commonly affected site. PAD shares its underlying pathophysiology and risk factors with coronary artery disease, as both are manifestations of the same systemic atherosclerotic process.

Summary Pearls — PAD / PVD

Definition & Pathophysiology

Left-to-right shunts describe congenital heart disease resulting in shunting of oxygenated blood from the left heart to the right heart — diseases include ventricular septal defect (VSD), atrial septal defect (ASD), patent ductus arteriosus (PDA), and coarctation of the aorta. Right-to-left shunts describe congenital heart disease resulting in shunting of deoxygenated blood from the right heart directly into the systemic (left-sided) circulation, bypassing the lungs. Because oxygenated blood normally moves left to right initially (acyanotic), but deoxygenated blood entering the systemic circulation in right-to-left shunts produces visible cyanosis, shunt direction is the key determinant of clinical cyanosis.

Eisenmenger Syndrome — Shunt Reversal

Eisenmenger syndrome results from uncorrected left-to-right shunting, which over time causes pulmonary arterial hypertension and right ventricular hypertrophy. As pulmonary vascular resistance progressively rises and eventually exceeds systemic vascular resistance, the shunt direction REVERSES from left-to-right to right-to-left — at this point, the patient who was previously acyanotic develops late cyanosis ("tet spells" do not apply here; this is a distinct late complication of uncorrected acyanotic lesions). This is why a previously acyanotic congenital heart disease patient (VSD, ASD, PDA) can present with new cyanosis in later childhood or adulthood if the defect was never repaired.

Summary Pearls — Shunts
  • Left-to-right = acyanotic at birth (the "3 Ds": VSD, ASD, PDA, plus coarctation). Right-to-left = cyanotic at birth ("blue babies," classic cyanotic lesions).
  • VSD is the most common congenital heart defect overall; symptoms emerge as physiologic PVR drops after birth, unmasking the left-to-right gradient.
  • PDA: continuous "machinery murmur." Can be life-sustaining (duct-dependent) in cyanotic lesions — closing it would be fatal until surgical repair.
  • Indomethacin (NSAID) closes a PDA pharmacologically; prostaglandin E1 keeps a PDA open — opposite mechanisms, both clinically important depending on the lesion.
  • Eisenmenger syndrome: chronic uncorrected L→R shunt → pulmonary hypertension → shunt reversal to R→L → late-onset cyanosis. A classic "don't miss" complication of untreated acyanotic CHD.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Cyanotic congenital heart defects (CHDs) are congenital cardiac malformations characterized by a right-to-left cardiac shunt, leading to deoxygenated blood entering the systemic circulation; the resulting hypoxemia manifests clinically as cyanosis. Common causes include genetic defects (e.g., DiGeorge syndrome/22q11 deletion), maternal conditions (e.g., maternal diabetes), and spontaneous genetic mutations. Acyanotic CHDs occur as isolated defects or in combination with others; individuals are generally asymptomatic or present with signs of heart failure (exercise intolerance, shortness of breath, poor feeding, failure to thrive in infants) rather than cyanosis, unless the lesion later progresses to Eisenmenger physiology.

Summary Pearls — Cyanotic vs Acyanotic CHD
  • Tetralogy of Fallot = most common cyanotic CHD; 4 features (RVOTO + VSD + overriding aorta + RVH); boot-shaped heart on CXR; squatting relieves tet spells by increasing SVR.
  • "5 T's" of cyanotic CHD: Tetralogy of Fallot, Transposition of the great arteries, Truncus arteriosus, Tricuspid atresia, TAPVR.
  • "3 Ds" of acyanotic CHD (by frequency): VSD > ASD > PDA — plus coarctation of the aorta.
  • Transposition of the great arteries is incompatible with life without an associated shunt allowing blood mixing — needs urgent intervention (balloon atrial septostomy, prostaglandin E1, then arterial switch/Jatene procedure).
  • Squatting increases systemic vascular resistance → reduces right-to-left shunting → improves oxygenation in TOF tet spells. This is the opposite hemodynamic effect of squatting on most other murmurs (e.g., it INCREASES the intensity of most murmurs by increasing preload/afterload, but here the benefit is via shunt physiology, not murmur intensity).
  • Eisenmenger syndrome can convert an originally acyanotic lesion into a cyanotic presentation later in life if left uncorrected.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Atherosclerosis is a chronic disease involving plaque buildup within arterial walls, leading to narrowed vessels and reduced blood flow. Plaques are composed of lipids, cholesterol, calcium, and inflammatory cells, contributing to progressive vessel obstruction and increasing the risk of ischemic events like myocardial infarction. Vulnerable plaques have thin fibrous caps and large lipid cores, making them prone to rupture, exposing thrombogenic material that triggers clot formation and acute coronary syndrome (ACS). All ACSs are caused by plaque rupture and thrombosis, requiring antithrombotic (heparin) and antiplatelet (aspirin, clopidogrel) drug therapy. Stable angina pathophysiology is similar to chronic mesenteric ischemia and intermittent vascular claudication — a fixed, flow-limiting stenosis causing demand-supply mismatch only with exertion.

  • Plaque rupture (not gradual stenosis progression) is the proximate trigger for acute coronary syndromes — this is why ACS can occur even in arteries with only moderate baseline stenosis if the plaque is "vulnerable" (thin cap, large lipid core).

Definition, Pathophysiology & Rheumatic Heart Disease

Heart murmurs are abnormal sounds created by turbulent blood flow across defective cardiac valves. Key causes of valvular damage: endocarditis (infection of the endocardium commonly affecting valvular leaflets), calcification deposits on valve leaflets (age-related degenerative process), rheumatic fever (an autoimmune disease typically associated with prior Group A streptococcal infection — caused by immune cross-reactivity, a type II hypersensitivity mechanism), and congenital valve defects. Rheumatic fever is diagnosed using the Jones criteria: Joints (migratory polyarthritis), Carditis, Nodules (subcutaneous), Erythema marginatum, Sydenham chorea. Acute rheumatic carditis can affect all valves, but the LATE sequela of rheumatic heart disease is classically mitral stenosis, from chronic post-inflammatory thickening and fusion of valve leaflets.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Infective endocarditis (IE) is a serious, life-threatening infection involving the endocardial surface of the heart, most commonly the valves, characterized by the formation of vegetations composed of fibrin, platelets, microorganisms, and inflammatory cells. Bacteremia seeds damaged or abnormal endocardium (native valve disease, prosthetic material, or congenital defects), where organisms adhere and proliferate within a protective fibrin-platelet matrix, relatively shielded from host immune defenses and antibiotics — this is why prolonged IV antibiotic courses are required for cure.

Hyperlipidemia — Definition & Classification

Hyperlipidemias are characterized by elevated lipid levels in the blood. Hyperlipidemia is often asymptomatic but significantly increases the risk for cardiovascular disease, especially atherosclerosis. Classification can follow primary (genetic) versus secondary (acquired) causes, with primary lipidemias often exacerbated by secondary factors. Lipoproteins comprise proteins and phospholipids that transport cholesterol and triglycerides in the body; apolipoproteins on the lipoprotein surface (such as ApoB-100, ApoB-48, ApoA-I) mediate receptor binding and enzyme activation.

Constrictive Pericarditis (Chronic Complication)

Constrictive pericarditis results from chronic pericardial fibrosis and scarring (often following recurrent or untreated acute pericarditis, prior cardiac surgery, or radiation), causing diastolic dysfunction as the rigid, non-compliant pericardium restricts ventricular filling. Unlike tamponade (an acute compressive process from fluid), constrictive pericarditis is a chronic, fibrotic restrictive process — but both share the physiology of impaired diastolic filling and can present with similar systemic congestive symptoms (elevated JVP, edema, ascites). A pericardial knock may be heard, and imaging shows pericardial thickening/calcification.

⚠ Cardiac tamponade is an immediate hemodynamic emergency — Beck's triad (hypotension + muffled heart sounds + JVD) plus pulsus paradoxus in a patient with a known or suspected pericardial effusion should prompt urgent echocardiography and emergent pericardiocentesis. Do not delay for extensive workup if the patient is hemodynamically unstable.
Summary Pearls — Pericarditis & Tamponade
  • Pericarditis: pleuritic chest pain that improves leaning FORWARD, diffuse concave ST elevation + PR depression on ECG, friction rub. First-line: NSAIDs + colchicine.
  • Tamponade: Beck's triad (hypotension, muffled heart sounds, JVD) + pulsus paradoxus. Echo shows RA/RV diastolic collapse. Treatment = emergent pericardiocentesis.
  • Pulsus paradoxus = exaggerated (>10mmHg) drop in systolic BP with inspiration — classic for tamponade, but also seen in severe asthma/COPD and constrictive pericarditis.
  • Coxsackievirus is the most common identifiable cause of acute pericarditis; most cases overall are idiopathic/presumed viral.
  • Constrictive pericarditis = chronic fibrotic restriction (vs tamponade = acute fluid compression) — both impair diastolic filling but via different mechanisms and timelines.
  • Dressler syndrome: autoimmune pericarditis occurring weeks after MI or cardiac surgery — a distinct, delayed-onset cause to recognize in a post-MI patient with new pleuritic chest pain.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Accumulation of air within the pleural space → loss of the negative intrapleural pressure that normally holds the lung expanded against the chest wall → partial or complete lung collapse. Definition & Pathophysiology

Chronic inflammatory disorder of the airways characterized by reversible bronchoconstriction, airway hyperresponsiveness, and remodeling. Type 2 (Th2-mediated) inflammation in allergic asthma: allergen exposure → Th2 cells release IL-4, IL-5, IL-13 → IgE production by B cells → mast cell sensitization → re-exposure triggers mast cell degranulation → histamine, leukotrienes, prostaglandins released → bronchoconstriction, mucus hypersecretion, airway edema. Early phase (minutes, bronchospasm) and late phase (4-6 hrs, eosinophilic inflammation) reactions occur. Chronic inflammation → airway remodeling: smooth muscle hypertrophy, subepithelial fibrosis, goblet cell hyperplasia.

  • ICS is the controller backbone; SABA is rescue only. Overuse of SABA without controller therapy is a marker of poor control.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Progressive, largely irreversible airflow limitation from two overlapping processes: emphysema (destruction of alveolar walls and interalveolar septa → loss of elastic recoil → air trapping → ↑RV/TLC) and chronic bronchitis (chronic inflammation and mucus gland hyperplasia in the airways → productive cough). Cigarette smoke → activates neutrophils and macrophages → release of proteases (elastase) that overwhelm antiprotease defenses (alpha-1 antitrypsin) → progressive destruction of lung parenchyma. Diagnostic criterion: post-bronchodilator FEV1/FVC <0.70 (GOLD 2024). Smoking cessation is the only intervention proven to slow the rate of FEV1 decline.

  • FEV1/FVC <0.7 post-bronchodilator with LITTLE TO NO reversibility = COPD (vs significant reversibility in asthma).

  • Pink puffer (emphysema) vs blue bloater (chronic bronchitis) — classic but imperfect clinical caricatures; most patients have features of both.
  • Smoking cessation is the only intervention that changes the trajectory of FEV1 decline — pharmacotherapy improves symptoms but not the underlying decline rate.

Definition & Pathophysiology

A group of disorders involving chronic inflammation and progressive fibrosis of the lung interstitium → thickened alveolar walls → impaired gas exchange + reduced lung compliance → restrictive physiology. Persistent alveolar injury (from toxins, immune dysregulation, or unknown cause) → fibroblast activation → excessive collagen deposition → irreversible scarring. Definition & Pathophysiology

Hearing loss resulting from a disruption in the transmission of sound waves through the external ear canal or middle ear (ossicular chain) before they reach the cochlea. The outer and middle ear normally mediate "conductive" hearing — any blockage, perforation, fluid, or fixation along this pathway impairs sound from ever reaching the functioning inner ear. Because the cochlea and auditory nerve remain intact, the inner ear becomes relatively MORE sensitive to bone-conducted sound, which is the physiologic basis for the Weber test lateralizing to the affected ear.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Hearing loss caused by dysfunction of the inner ear (cochlea — sensory) or the auditory/vestibulocochlear nerve (CN VIII) and its central pathways (neural). Because sound is normally transmitted via air conduction more efficiently than bone conduction in a healthy ear, and SNHL affects the cochlea/nerve equally regardless of conduction route, the AC>BC ratio (Rinne positive) is preserved even on the affected side — but overall hearing is reduced. Sensory loss (cochlear/hair cell damage) is sometimes partially reversible; neural loss (CN VIII or central pathway) is rarely recoverable and may signal a serious underlying lesion such as a cerebellopontine angle tumor.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Autosomal recessive disorder from mutations in the CFTR gene (chromosome 7), encoding a cAMP-regulated chloride channel on epithelial cells. Defective CFTR → decreased chloride (and bicarbonate) secretion + increased sodium/water reabsorption across epithelial surfaces → thick, dehydrated, viscous secretions in the lungs, pancreas, GI tract, sweat glands, and reproductive tract. In the lungs: mucus accumulation impairs mucociliary clearance → recurrent infections (Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Staphylococcus aureus, Burkholderia cepacia) → chronic inflammation → bronchiectasis and progressive fibrosis. The most common mutation, ΔF508 (Class II), causes a misfolded CFTR protein that is degraded before reaching the cell surface.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Malignant neoplasm arising from bronchial or alveolar epithelium, broadly divided into small cell lung cancer (SCLC, ~15%) and non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC, ~85%) — the latter comprising adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and large cell carcinoma. Pathogenesis: carcinogen exposure (tobacco smoke is dominant) → genetic mutations (EGFR, ALK, KRAS oncogene activation; TP53 tumor suppressor inactivation) → uncontrolled epithelial proliferation. Local invasion into bronchi, lung tissue, and chest wall; lymphatic spread to mediastinal nodes; distant metastases classically to liver, brain, bone, and adrenal glands.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Malignant clonal plasma cells secrete M protein (IgG 55%, IgA 25%) + excess free light chains (Bence Jones proteins). Plasma cells stimulate osteoclasts via RANKL/IL-6 and suppress osteoblasts via DKK-1 → lytic bone lesions without reactive sclerosis. They crowd normal marrow → cytopenias and suppress normal Ig → immunodeficiency. Bence Jones proteins → nephrotoxic tubular damage → renal failure (myeloma kidney).

Definition & Pathophysiology

Malignant lymphoma defined by Reed-Sternberg (RS) cells — large binucleated cells with prominent "owl-eye" eosinophilic nucleoli — expressing CD30+ / CD15+ but lacking CD45 (LCA) and usually CD20. Derived from germinal center B cells. EBV associated with mixed cellularity subtype. RS cells are a minority; surrounding reactive microenvironment drives symptoms via cytokine release (IL-5 → eosinophilia; IL-6 → fever; IL-13 → pruritus). Spreads contiguously (node-to-node, orderly).

Definition & Classification Framework

Anemia is a reduction in hemoglobin concentration (WHO: Hgb <12 g/dL in women, <13 g/dL in men) leading to decreased oxygen delivery. The diagnostic approach uses two axes: MCV (cell size) and reticulocyte count (bone marrow response). Summary: Is the reticulocyte count high (destruction/loss) or low (production failure)? Step 2: Use MCV to classify production failures into micro-, normo-, or macrocytic subtypes.

Microcytic (MCV <80 fL)

Mnemonic: TAILS
Thalassemia · Anemia of chronic disease · Iron deficiency · Lead poisoning · Sideroblastic anemia


Mechanism: deficient heme or globin synthesis → small, pale RBCs

Normocytic (MCV 80–100 fL)

Causes: Acute blood loss, hemolytic anemias, anemia of chronic disease (early), aplastic anemia, renal failure (↓EPO), hypothyroidism, bone marrow infiltration


Key: check reticulocyte count to separate high (hemolysis/loss) from low (production failure)

Macrocytic (MCV >100 fL)

Megaloblastic: B12 deficiency, folate deficiency (DNA synthesis defect)
Non-megaloblastic: alcohol, liver disease, hypothyroidism, MDS, reticulocytosis, drugs (MTX, hydroxyurea)

Reticulocyte Count — The Key Branch Point
High reticulocytes (>2–3%) = Appropriate BM response
Bone marrow is working fine but RBCs are being lost or destroyed. Think: hemorrhage (acute blood loss) or hemolysis (intrinsic or extrinsic RBC destruction).
Low reticulocytes (<2%) = Inadequate BM response
Bone marrow is failing to produce enough RBCs. Classify by MCV → microcytic (TAILS), normocytic (aplastic, renal failure), or macrocytic (B12/folate, liver, alcohol).

Summary Pearls — Anemia General
  • First branch point: Is the reticulocyte count high (loss/destruction) or low (production failure)?
  • Second branch point (if low retics): What is the MCV? Microcytic → TAILS; Normocytic → aplastic/renal/early IDA; Macrocytic → B12/folate/alcohol/liver.
  • Hemolysis findings: high retics + ↑indirect bilirubin + ↑LDH + ↓haptoglobin.
  • RDW ↑ in iron deficiency and B12/folate deficiency (anisocytosis); RDW normal in thalassemia trait (uniform small cells).
  • Early iron deficiency can be normocytic — MCV drops only after prolonged depletion.

Summary Pearls — Microcytic Anemias
  • IDA: ferritin↓ (first to deplete) + TIBC↑ + serum iron↓. Ferritin is the most sensitive early marker.
  • ACD: serum iron↓ but ferritin↑ + TIBC↓ — iron is trapped in macrophages by hepcidin. The key distinguisher from IDA.
  • Sideroblastic anemia: serum iron↑ + ferritin↑ + ringed sideroblasts on bone marrow. INH → give B6. Lead → give DMSA/EDTA.
  • Thalassemia trait: microcytic anemia with NORMAL iron studies + ELEVATED RBC count + target cells on smear. Confirm with Hgb electrophoresis.
  • Basophilic stippling with ringed sideroblasts = sideroblastic anemia. Basophilic stippling WITHOUT ringed sideroblasts = lead poisoning.
  • Pica + koilonychia + smooth tongue + esophageal webs (Plummer-Vinson) = Iron deficiency anemia.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Megaloblastic anemia = defect in DNA synthesis → delayed nuclear maturation relative to cytoplasmic maturation → large cells with immature nuclei (megaloblasts in marrow; macro-ovalocytes and hypersegmented neutrophils in blood). Caused by deficiency of Vitamin B12 or Folate — both required for thymidine synthesis (via methylation pathways). Non-megaloblastic macrocytosis (no DNA synthesis defect): alcohol, liver disease, hypothyroidism, reticulocytosis, MDS, drugs.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Thalassemias are inherited autosomal recessive disorders of globin chain synthesis. Reduced production of one chain type → relative excess of the other → excess chains precipitate → damage RBC membrane → hemolysis and ineffective erythropoiesis. Iron studies are normal (iron absorption intact), which is the key distinguisher from iron deficiency anemia. Compensatory: ↑HbA2 and HbF in beta-thalassemia; bone marrow expands; extramedullary hematopoiesis.

Summary Pearls — Thalassemia
  • Thalassemia trait: microcytic anemia + NORMAL iron studies + ELEVATED RBC count + target cells. Confirm with Hgb electrophoresis.
  • Beta-thal major: presents 6–12 months when HbF fades. Frontal bossing + crew-cut skull X-ray (marrow expansion) + hepatosplenomegaly + transfusion-dependent.
  • Alpha-thal: 4 genes, 4 presentations. 4 deleted = hydrops fetalis (incompatible with life). Normal electrophoresis in trait (2 genes deleted).
  • Do NOT give iron to thalassemia trait — iron studies are normal. Iron supplementation causes iron overload.
  • Target cells mnemonic HALT: HbC disease, Asplenia, Liver disease, Thalassemia.
  • Iron overload from chronic transfusions: chelation with deferoxamine or deferasirox prevents cardiomyopathy and cirrhosis.

Definition & Pathophysiology

Single nucleotide substitution in beta-globin gene: GAG→GTG → Glutamate→Valine at position 6. HbS under deoxygenated conditions forms insoluble polymers → sickling → (1) vaso-occlusion: rigid sickled RBCs + endothelial adhesion + leukocytes → microvascular occlusion → ischemia/infarction; (2) hemolysis: sickled cells destroyed in spleen → chronic normocytic hemolytic anemia. Triggers: hypoxia, dehydration, acidosis, cold, infection. HbS trait (HbAS) is protective against P. falciparum malaria.

  • Triggers for sickling: Hypoxia, Dehydration, Acidosis, Cold, Infection — remember HDACI.
  • Dactylitis (hand-foot syndrome) = first presentation of SCD in children under 2 years old.
  • Parvovirus B19 → aplastic crisis (shuts down erythroid precursors → sudden severe anemia).
  • Salmonella species → osteomyelitis in SCD (S. aureus still most common in general population).
  • Autosplenectomy by age 5 → Howell-Jolly bodies → give prophylactic penicillin + vaccines (pneumococcal, meningococcal, Hib).
  • Hydroxyurea increases HbF → HbF inhibits HbS polymerization → fewer crises.
  • Acute chest syndrome = leading cause of death in SCD → exchange transfusion is key.

Definition & Life Cycle

Malaria is caused by Plasmodium protozoa transmitted by the bite of female Anopheles mosquitoes. Five species infect humans: P. falciparum, P. vivax, P. ovale, P. malariae, P. knowlesi. Life cycle: (1) female Anopheles injects sporozoites → (2) sporozoites travel to liver → hepatic schizogony (asymptomatic) → (3) liver schizonts rupture, releasing merozoites → (4) merozoites infect RBCs → erythrocytic schizogony → (5) schizont rupture → fever/symptoms + release of new merozoites. P. vivax and P. ovale form dormant hypnozoites in liver → reactivate weeks to years later → relapses.

Summary Pearls — Malaria
  • P. falciparum: most severe; banana-shaped gametocytes; multiple ring forms per RBC; no hypnozoites; cerebral malaria; blackwater fever (hemoglobinuria).
  • P. vivax and P. ovale: hypnozoites in liver → relapses months/years later → primaquine eradicates liver stage. Check G6PD first!
  • P. malariae: quartan fever (72h cycle); nephrotic syndrome; infects senescent RBCs; no hypnozoites.
  • Schüffner's dots on smear = P. vivax or P. ovale. Banana-shaped gametocytes = P. falciparum.
  • Duffy antigen is required for P. vivax RBC invasion → Duffy-negative individuals (West Africans) are naturally resistant to P. vivax.
  • Sickle cell trait and G6PD deficiency are protective against P. falciparum malaria.
  • IV artesunate preferred over IV quinine for severe falciparum (better efficacy, fewer side effects).

Coagulation Cascade Overview
Extrinsic Pathway (PT/INR)
Tissue factor (III) + Factor VII → activates Factor X. "SITS" — Seven Is The Start. PT/INR elevated if factors in this pathway are deficient (VII, X, V, II, fibrinogen). Warfarin blocks vitamin K-dependent factors (II, VII, IX, X, Protein C, S).
Intrinsic Pathway (PTT)
Contact activation → XII → XI → IX → VIII → X. "PET" → PTT tests Eight and nine (IXa + VIIIa activate X). PTT elevated if: hemophilia A (VIII↓), hemophilia B (IX↓), heparin (activates antithrombin → inactivates IIa/Xa). vWF carries VIII — vWD can ↑PTT.
Common Pathway (both PT & PTT)
Factor X → Xa (with Va) → prothrombin (II) → thrombin (IIa) → fibrinogen (I) → fibrin. XIII crosslinks fibrin. Deficiency here affects both PT and PTT.
Primary Hemostasis (Bleeding Time)
Platelet plug formation: vascular injury → vWF → platelet GpIb binding (adhesion) → ADP/TXA2 → platelet aggregation (GpIIb/IIIa). BT elevated in: platelet disorders, vWD, aspirin/NSAIDs, uremia.

Summary Pearls — Coagulation Cascade
  • PT tests extrinsic (VII) and common pathway. PTT tests intrinsic (VIII, IX, XI, XII) and common pathway.
  • Vitamin K-dependent factors: II, VII, IX, X, Protein C, S (mnemonic: "1972" → factors 2,7,9,10).
  • Factor VII has the shortest half-life → PT/INR elevated first in warfarin therapy and liver disease.
  • TTP: ADAMTS13 deficiency → ultralarge vWF multimers → microthrombi → FAT RN pentad: Fever, microAngiopathic hemolytic anemia (schistocytes), Thrombocytopenia, Renal failure, Neurologic changes. Treat with plasmapheresis.
  • DIC: EVERYTHING is consumed → PT↑, PTT↑, fibrinogen↓, platelets↓, D-dimer↑↑. Causes: sepsis, trauma, obstetric complications, malignancy.
  • Mixing study: mix patient plasma with normal plasma. If PTT corrects → factor DEFICIENCY (hemophilia). If PTT does NOT correct → INHIBITOR (autoantibody against factor).

Definition & Pathophysiology

Hemophilia A and B are X-linked recessive disorders — almost exclusively males affected (females are carriers, occasionally symptomatic). Hemophilia A: deficiency of Factor VIII; most common (80% of hemophilias). Hemophilia B (Christmas disease): deficiency of Factor IX. Both factors are in the intrinsic pathway → IXa + VIIIa together activate Factor X. Deficiency → inadequate thrombin generation → failure to form stable fibrin clot → bleeding into deep tissues (joints, muscles). Both are clinically indistinguishable — only factor assay differentiates them. ~1/3 of cases arise from spontaneous mutation (no family history).

Summary Pearls — Hemophilia A & B
  • X-linked recessive — almost exclusively in males. Females are carriers (may have mild symptoms).
  • Classic presentation: young boy with hemarthroses (joint bleeds) + elevated PTT + normal PT + normal bleeding time.
  • Hemophilia A = Factor VIII↓ (most common). Hemophilia B = Factor IX↓ (Christmas disease). Clinically identical — distinguish by factor assay.
  • DDAVP (desmopressin): releases stored vWF and Factor VIII from endothelium → effective for mild Hemophilia A and vWD Type 1. Not useful for B.
  • Mixing study: PTT corrects with normal plasma = factor DEFICIENCY (hemophilia). PTT does NOT correct = INHIBITOR (autoantibody).
  • Acquired hemophilia: autoantibodies against FVIII develop in SLE, pregnancy, malignancy → PTT elevated, mixing study does NOT correct. Treat with corticosteroids + emicizumab.

Other Thrombophilias & Hypercoagulable States
Prothrombin G20210A mutation
2nd most common inherited thrombophilia in Caucasians. Elevated prothrombin levels → increased thrombin generation → DVT/PE risk. Normal PT/PTT. Autosomal dominant.
Protein C & S deficiency
Natural anticoagulants. Deficiency → inadequate inactivation of Va and VIIIa → hypercoagulable. Warfarin-induced skin necrosis (Protein C short half-life, drops first). Autosomal dominant.
Antithrombin III deficiency
AT-III normally inhibits thrombin and Xa. Deficiency → hypercoagulable. Heparin resistance (heparin requires AT-III to work). Acquired by nephrotic syndrome, liver disease, heparin use.
Antiphospholipid syndrome (APS)
Autoimmune: antiphospholipid antibodies → paradoxical hypercoagulable state (thrombosis + recurrent miscarriages) despite ↑PTT in vitro. Lupus anticoagulant; anticardiolipin Ab; anti-β2 glycoprotein I. Treatment: lifelong anticoagulation.
Warfarin-induced skin necrosis: occurs early in warfarin therapy due to rapid depletion of Protein C (short half-life, vitamin K-dependent) before anticoagulant effect is established → paradoxical thrombosis of skin microvasculature → necrosis. Seen especially in Protein C/S deficiency. Overlap with heparin when starting warfarin to prevent this.
Summary Pearls — Acquired Disorders
  • ITP: isolated thrombocytopenia + normal PT/PTT + increased megakaryocytes on marrow. Treat with steroids → IVIG → splenectomy. Do NOT transfuse platelets unless emergency.
  • TTP: pentad (FAT RN) + schistocytes + NORMAL PT/PTT (not DIC!). ADAMTS13 deficiency. Treat with PLASMAPHERESIS — do NOT give platelets.
  • HUS vs TTP: HUS = E. coli O157:H7 (Shiga toxin) + hemolytic anemia + renal failure + thrombocytopenia, primarily children. Treatment: supportive (avoid antibiotics in Shiga-HUS).
  • DIC: ALL consumed (PT↑, PTT↑, fibrinogen↓, platelets↓, D-dimer↑↑). Treat the underlying cause FIRST.
  • Factor V Leiden: most common inherited thrombophilia in Caucasians. Normal PT/PTT. Resistance to APC. DVT/PE/miscarriages. Worsened by OCP.
  • vWD: mucosal bleeding (NOT hemarthroses). ↑bleeding time + ↓ristocetin cofactor activity. DDAVP for Type 1. Most common inherited bleeding disorder overall.
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome: paradox — ↑PTT in vitro but HYPERCOAGULABLE in vivo. Recurrent thrombosis + miscarriages. Treat with lifelong anticoagulation.
